The Star That Went Dark: The Political
Assassination of MedStar and the High
Cost of Fort Worth’s "Modernization"
By Lois Lane Special Investigative Report

Chapter I: The Crisis Under Our Feet
It was not a death in the night. It was not a sudden cardiac arrest that stopped the heart of
Tarrant County’s emergency medical system. It was a slow, calculated strangulation, performed
in the fluorescent glare of city council chambers and buried under mountains of consulting
reports.
For thirty-eight years, the Metropolitan Area Emergency Medical Services Authority—known to
the people of Fort Worth simply as MedStar—was the thin blue and white line between life and
death for over a million people. It was a system that, by every objective metric of the industry,
worked. It was "World Class". It was the lowest-cost provider in the state of Texas for a decade.
It was an agency that pioneered the very concept of Mobile Integrated Healthcare, saving the
healthcare system millions by treating the sick in their living rooms instead of hauling them to
overcrowded emergency departments.
And on July 1, 2025, it was erased.
The ambulances were repainted. The "Star of Life" that had adorned the fleet for nearly four
decades was stripped away, replaced by the branding of the Fort Worth Fire Department
(FWFD). The workforce—a dedicated cadre of over 600 paramedics, EMTs, and
dispatchers—was absorbed into the paramilitary structure of the fire service, their unique
cultural identity dissolved into the red-brick tradition of the firehouse.
To the public, this was sold as a rescue mission. City Hall and Fire Command spun a narrative
of "sustainability," "stabilization," and "modernization". They told you the system was broken.
They told you the finances were dire. They told you that the only way to save EMS in Fort Worth
was to hand the keys to the Fire Department.
They lied.
What happened in Fort Worth was not a medical necessity; it was a political acquisition. It is a
story of a city that starved a high-performing agency of necessary funds for over a decade, only
to declare it "unsustainable" when the inevitable malnutrition set in. It is the story of a transition
plan that replaces a lean, data-driven deployment model with a bloated, station-based
architecture projected to cost taxpayers tens of millions more annually while relying on a foreign
private contractor to handle the "boring" work.
This is not just a change in logo. It is a fundamental restructuring of the social contract between
the city and its sickest citizens. It is a shift from healthcare to public safety, from efficiency to
bureaucracy, and from a public utility to a political asset.
We are standing on the grave of one of the most efficient EMS systems in America, and we are
paying $85 million a year for the shovel.
Chapter II: The Structural Origin Sin
To understand how we arrived at this autopsy, one must first understand the anatomy of the
victim. MedStar was born from the chaos of the "ambulance wars" of the 1980s, established as
a Public Utility Model (PUM). This structure was a stroke of civic genius: a quasi-governmental
authority designed to strip the profit motive from emergency medicine while insulating it from the
lethargy of municipal bureaucracy.
For decades, the deal was simple: MedStar provided elite-level care, and in return, it asked for
nothing from the city's general fund.

The Zero-Subsidy Miracle
In an era where police and fire departments consumed nearly half of municipal budgets,
MedStar was a unicorn. For over ten years prior to the crisis of 2023, MedStar required zero
public subsidy from the City of Fort Worth. It operated entirely on a "user fee" model, funding
its operations through insurance reimbursements and collections.
It subsidized the city’s safety net by existing. Every heart attack treated, every car crash
managed, every stroke reversed—MedStar paid for it with its own revenue.

The "Structural Origin Sin" was not that MedStar was flawed, but that it was too efficient for its
own good. By asking for nothing, it allowed City Hall to become complacent. The city grew used
to getting premium EMS for free. So when the economic tectonic plates shifted post-2020, the
city wasn't ready to pay the bill that every other municipality in America had been paying for
years.

Chapter III: The Long, Quiet Decline
The rot didn't start in the ambulance bays; it started in the ledger. The post-pandemic economy
was a perfect storm for EMS agencies nationwide, and MedStar was standing in the rain without
an umbrella.

The Economic suffocation
Inflation drove up the cost of diesel, medical supplies, and ambulances. Supply chain shortages
meant that ordering a new rig took two years instead of six months. But the real killer was labor.
To retain paramedics in a burning labor market—where nurses were getting $100/hour travel
contracts—MedStar had to increase wages by nearly 58% over eighteen months just to keep
the ambulances staffed.
Simultaneously, the revenue side collapsed. Commercial insurance reimbursements stagnated.
Federal payments from Medicare and Medicaid were slashed. The "uncompensated care"
burden—the cost of treating the uninsured and the homeless—skyrocketed. Council member
Carlos Flores noted that for many, MedStar had become a "default health care plan," a dynamic
that was financially unsustainable for a fee-for-service model.
In 2023, MedStar finally broke the silence. They approached the City of Fort Worth—their
primary stakeholder—and asked for help. They requested a subsidy of approximately $6 million
to stabilize the ship.
To put this in perspective: $6 million is a rounding error in the City of Fort Worth’s budget. It is a
fraction of what is spent on economic incentives for corporate relocations. It is pennies on the
dollar compared to the police budget.
But the City’s response was not to bolster its partner. It was to interrogate them.

The "Bailout" Narrative
The request for funding was met with political hostility. Council Member Jared Williams went on
record stating, "I do not support footing the whole bill for a temporary bailout".
"Bailout."
The word was a weapon. It framed MedStar not as a critical public service akin to the water
department or the police, but as a failing business looking for a handout. It fundamentally
misrepresented the nature of EMS economics. Police and Fire departments are 100%
subsidized by the taxpayer; they do not generate revenue to cover their costs. They are cost
centers. Yet when the ambulance service, which had subsidized the city for years by not taking
tax dollars, finally asked for parity, it was treated as a failure of management.
The City Council approved a "transitional" stopgap of $4.2 million for FY2024, but it came with a
poison pill: a comprehensive third-party study to evaluate "solutions". This was the beginning of
the end. By refusing to commit to long-term reliable funding, the City ensured that MedStar’s
reserves would continue to dwindle, forcing the very "instability" that the Ad Hoc Committee was
formed to "solve".
It was a classic hostile takeover tactic: devalue the asset, declare it distressed, starve it of cash,
and then acquire it for parts.

Chapter IV: The Consultants and the Predetermined
Verdict
Enter Fitch & Associates.
In October 2023, the city engaged this consulting firm to conduct an "EMS Comprehensive
Study". In the world of municipal consulting, the phrasing of the question often dictates the
answer. The mandate was to find a model that ensured "long-term viability" and "stability."
Fitch presented four models to the Ad Hoc Committee.
   1.​ Retain and Fund MedStar: This model would have cost the city an estimated $3.2
       million annually in subsidy.
   2.​ Fire-Based EMS: This model was estimated to cost significantly more—initial estimates
       ranged widely but settled on a massive budget expansion.
The data was clear: MedStar was the most economically efficient option. The Fitch report itself
acknowledged that MedStar had been the lowest-cost provider in Texas for a decade.
Yet, the Ad Hoc Committee, heavily lobbied by the Fort Worth Professional Firefighters
Association (IAFF Local 440), leaned into the narrative that a "unified" command would be
superior. The Fitch report noted potential "higher potential costs due to use of sworn personnel"
, but this warning was drowned out by the seductive promise of "sustainability."
The fix was in. The decision to dissolve MedStar and hand the keys to the FWFD was ratified in
May 2024. The rationale was that a fire-based system would be "financially sustainable."
Let us look at that "sustainability." The projected budget for the new FWFD EMS division is $85
million. Projected revenue is $65 million. That leaves a $20 million annual deficit to be
covered by the General Fund.
The city refused to give MedStar $6 million to stay independent and world-class, but it happily
signed a check for $20 million a year to the Fire Department for the same service. This is not
fiscal responsibility. It is political patronage.

Chapter V: The Human Ledger
While the politicians debated budgets, the people on the streets were paying the price.
To understand what was lost, you have to look past the spreadsheets and into the "human
ledger." The men and women of MedStar were not just employees; they were a tribe. They
worked in a "Third Service" culture—a distinct identity that was neither police nor fire. They were
clinicians first. They didn't have the downtime of firefighters. They didn't have the station
recliners. They lived their lives in the "box" (the ambulance), posted on street corners, eating
cold food, waiting for the radio to crackle.
The toll of this life is etched in the names of those who didn't make it to the pension.
The transition process itself was a trauma. For over a year, 600 employees lived in limbo, not
knowing if they would have jobs, if their seniority would count, or if their culture would survive.
When the deal was finally cut, it was a "merger" in name only. The Fort Worth Firefighters
Association (Local 440) negotiated a deal to bring the MedStar employees in, but they were
brought in as "single-role" providers.
This created a caste system.
   ●​ Dual-Role: The traditional firefighters. They ride the big red trucks. They fight fires. They
        have the heritage.
   ●​ Single-Role: The former MedStar crews. They ride the ambulances. They handle the
        "medical" calls (which are 80% of the volume). They are civil servants, but they are
        distinct.
The friction was immediate. During the transition, a bitter dispute flared over the dispatchers.
The union fought to have them classified as "sworn" personnel, while the city wanted them as
"civilians". This wasn't just about titles; it was about power, benefits, and the expansion of the
union's dues-paying base.
The "erasure" is visual. The MedStar uniforms—symbols of a specific kind of expertise—are
gone. They have been replaced by FWFD-branded "redesigned hoodies" and polos. It seems
like a small thing, but in the uniformed services, the patch on your shoulder is your identity. The
message was clear: You are no longer MedStar. You are ours now.

Chapter VI: The Corners Where the Truth Hid
One of the most critical casualties of this transition is the deployment model itself—a technical
detail with life-or-death consequences.
MedStar operated on System Status Management (SSM). In plain English: ambulances don't
sit in stations. They move. They are "posted" at street corners based on predictive data. If the
computer says there’s a high probability of a cardiac arrest in zip code 76104 at 5:00 PM on a
Friday, an ambulance is sitting there at 4:55 PM.
SSM is brutal on crews. It is uncomfortable. It is exhausting. But it is efficient. It shaves minutes
off response times because the ambulance is already halfway to the call before it comes in.
The FWFD model is Station-Based. Ambulances will largely operate out of fire stations.
   ●​ The Pro: Crews get access to dorms, kitchens, and bathrooms. Quality of life improves
        significantly.
   ●​ The Con: Fire stations are immobile. They were built decades ago based on the risk of
        structure fires, not the modern heat map of 911 medical calls.
An ambulance sitting in a fire station is often minutes further away from the patient than an
ambulance posted at a busy intersection. To achieve the same response time standards (8
minutes, 90% of the time) with a station-based model, you need significantly more ambulances
and more staff.
This is the hidden driver of the cost explosion. The city is trading efficiency for comfort. The
Fitch report recommended a ".50 Unit Hour Utilization" (UHU)—meaning crews should only be
busy 50% of the time. This is a luxury standard compared to the high-utilization model of
MedStar. It sounds humane, and perhaps it is, but the taxpayers are paying a premium for it,
and the response time reliability of the station-based model in a sprawling city like Fort Worth
remains a gamble.
The IAFF Local 440 produced its own GIS study to justify the station placements. But when the
entity advocating for more jobs conducts the study, the results rarely recommend doing more
with less. The shift to station-based deployment is a labor-friendly move that structurally inflates
the budget.

Chapter VII: The Trojan Horse of Privatization
If the Fire Department takeover was the headline, the arrival of Falck was the fine print that
should have terrified everyone.
While the city was celebrating the "public" takeover of EMS, it quietly opened the back door to a
private corporation. The City Council voted to privatize Interfacility Transports (IFTs) and "low
acuity" 911 calls.
Previously, MedStar handled everything. The revenue from lucrative IFTs (moving stable
patients between hospitals) helped cross-subsidize the money-losing 911 operations. This is
standard EMS economics: the profitable transfers pay for the unprofitable heart attacks.
By stripping IFTs out of the system, the city removed a critical revenue stream, guaranteeing
that the FWFD EMS division would run at a deficit forever.

The Outsider Entry
Who is Falck? Falck Mobile Health Corp is a Danish multinational giant. They began operations
in Fort Worth on June 17, 2025. Their mandate is to handle the non-emergency transfer work
and provide "911 backup."
The choice of Falck is alarming given their recent rap sheet in American cities.
   ●​ San Diego, CA: Falck was fined over $3 million for failing to meet response time targets
       and staffing requirements. The failure was so acute that the fire chief there requested the
       ability to subcontract to competitors just to get ambulances on the street.
   ●​ Alameda County, CA: Falck faced similar fines and was cited for "unacceptable" delays.
This is the partner Fort Worth has chosen to "alleviate" pressure on the system. The introduction
of a private contractor introduces the volatility of the free market into the safety net. Private EMS
companies are notorious for "abandoning" contracts when they become unprofitable. If Falck
decides Fort Worth isn't yielding the desired margin, they can pull out, leaving the city with a
massive gap in service coverage.
Furthermore, the new "permitting" system for IFTs effectively deregulates the market. It allows
private providers to scavenge for profitable transfers while avoiding the burden of emergency
readiness. This cannibalizes the system's ability to fund itself, transferring the entire financial
load of 911 readiness onto the taxpayer.

Chapter VIII: The National Mirror
Fort Worth is not unique. It is merely the latest domino in a national trend where fire
departments, faced with a decades-long decline in structural fires, absorb EMS systems to
justify their budgets and staffing levels.
In New York City, the FDNY takeover of NYC EMS in 1996 serves as a grim warning. Decades
later, FDNY EMS personnel are still fighting for pay parity, still treated as "second-class citizens"
in their own department, and still facing massive attrition issues. The culture war there never
ended; it just became a cold war.
The "Fire-Based" model is sold as efficiency—"one call, one team." But the reality is often a
bloat of resources. Fire departments are built for surge capacity (the big fire that happens once
a year). EMS is built for constant capacity (the steady drumbeat of sick people every hour).
Merging them often results in using expensive resources (fire engines) for low-acuity problems
(toothaches).
The adoption of the FWFD model in Fort Worth mirrors this pattern. It is an expansion of the fire
service's domain, fueled by the political power of the IAFF, rather than a data-driven decision
about patient care. The "efficiency" is a myth; the cost data proves it. We have replaced a $50
million problem with an $85 million solution.

Chapter IX: "Modernization" vs. Reality
Let us strip away the euphemisms.
"Modernization" meant deleting a data-driven deployment model (SSM) that was years ahead
of its time and replacing it with a station-based model that was obsolete in the 1990s.
"Sustainability" meant refusing to give a public utility $6 million and instead giving a city
department $20 million.
"Integration" meant dissolving the unique culture of a specialized medical service and forcing
its members into a hierarchy where they will arguably always be secondary to the "real"
firefighters.
The transition was executed with administrative competence—deadlines were hit, uniforms were
issued, radios were tuned. But operational competence does not excuse strategic malpractice.
The ship didn't sink during the handover, but it is now sailing on a course that is fundamentally
more expensive and arguably less effective than the one it replaced.
The Mobile Integrated Healthcare (MIH) program, once the envy of the nation, is promised to
continue. But MIH requires a mindset of prevention, of slow medicine, of social work. This is
antithetical to the "response" DNA of a fire department. It remains to be seen if the FWFD can
nurture a program that requires keeping people out of the system, when the fire service model is
built on responding to the system.

Chapter X: Closing Indictment
The collapse of MedStar was a choice.
It was a choice made by a City Council that preferred the political optics of a "unified" fire
department over the fiscal reality of a public utility. It was a choice to ignore the data that proved
MedStar was efficient. It was a choice to betray a workforce that had served consistently for 38
years, forcing them to trade their identity for their pensions.
And it was a choice to bring a private contractor with a history of failure into the heart of the
city's safety net, privatizing the profits while socializing the risks.
As the citizens of Fort Worth watch the newly branded red ambulances roll by, they should know
what they are looking at. They are not looking at "modernization." They are looking at a hostile
takeover. They are looking at the death of a star, and the rising cost of the fire that consumed it.
The ground is not level. The price has gone up. And the truth, as always, is buried in the budget
lines that nobody reads until the ambulance doesn't show up on time.
End of Report
