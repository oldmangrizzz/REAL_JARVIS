The Antitrust Implications of Generative
AI Gatekeeping: Collusion, Essential
Facilities, and Civil Rights Intersections
Executive Summary
The rapid commercialization of artificial intelligence (AI), specifically large language models
(LLMs) and the vast computational infrastructure required to train and deploy them, has
catalyzed an unprecedented concentration of global market power. A highly consolidated
oligopoly of technology conglomerates—frequently identified in economic literature as the
"GAMMANs" (Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Apple, and Nvidia)—now exercises profound,
unilateral control over the foundational layers of the modern AI ecosystem.1 This structural
concentration extends far beyond physical semiconductor hardware and hyperscale cloud
infrastructure; it permeates the model layer itself, creating systemic chokepoints that dominant
firms can leverage to dictate downstream market dynamics, stifle application development,
and, critically, suppress independent AI safety research.1
This exhaustive research report provides a deep-dive investigation into the federal antitrust
liability and regulatory enforcement mechanisms surrounding the collective gatekeeping of AI
infrastructure by major technology laboratories. The analysis synthesizes current jurisprudence
and economic theory to evaluate the legal peril of deploying exclusionary digital standards and
coordinated algorithmic behaviors. Specifically, the report addresses the legal standing of
uniform, undocumented "rolling limit" algorithmic throttling, unappealable automated support
walls, and discriminatory Application Programming Interface (API) revocations. Such practices
systematically box out independent and disabled AI safety researchers, thereby insulating
dominant, proprietary models from external oversight, rigorous ethical alignment testing, and
competitive pressure.
Through a detailed examination of the Sherman Antitrust Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1 & 2), the "Essential
Facilities" doctrine, Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) Act, and the novel,
emerging intersection of antitrust law with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and
Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, this report outlines the multi-front liability matrix facing
dominant AI gatekeepers. The synthesis of these doctrines reveals that the arbitrary exclusion
of independent auditors is not merely a matter of restrictive terms of service, but a potentially
illegal restraint of trade, an abuse of monopoly power, and a discriminatory enforcement of
digital infrastructure standards designed to maintain proprietary control over the global AI
safety narrative.
1. Algorithmic Collusion and the Sherman Antitrust
Act (Section 1)
Section 1 of the Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890 establishes the foundational prohibition against
"[e]very contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of
trade or commerce".3 To successfully prove a violation under Section 1, plaintiffs or federal
enforcers must traditionally demonstrate two primary elements: an agreement among
separate competitors, and evidence that the resulting agreement constitutes an unreasonable
restraint on trade.3 However, the proliferation of advanced AI systems and complex, opaque
algorithms has fundamentally transformed the mechanics of market coordination, severely
blurring the doctrinal lines between express collusion (which is per se illegal) and tacit collusion
or "conscious parallelism" (which is historically difficult to prosecute standing alone).3
Legal Standing for Hub-and-Spoke Conspiracies in Digital Markets

A central mechanism for prosecuting coordinated algorithmic behavior under Section 1 is the
"hub-and-spoke" conspiracy framework. This legal metaphor describes an antitrust cartel
structured identically to a wagon wheel: a vertically related firm (the "hub") enters into separate
vertical agreements (the "spokes") with various horizontal competitors operating at a different
level of the supply chain.3 Crucially, for this arrangement to violate Section 1, there must be a
discernible horizontal agreement among the spokes—the "rim" of the wheel—demonstrating a
shared, interdependent commitment to the anticompetitive scheme.4 Without the rim, the
structure is merely a series of vertical agreements, which are typically evaluated under the
more lenient "rule of reason" standard rather than being deemed per se illegal.3
The foundational precedent establishing this framework is the 1939 Supreme Court case
Interstate Circuit, Inc. v. United States, wherein a dominant movie theater exhibitor (acting as
the hub) sent identical written demands to eight competing film distributors (the spokes) to
enforce minimum ticket prices.3 The Court inferred a horizontal conspiracy because each
distributor had an independent economic motive to agree to the hub's demands only if they
possessed a tacit assurance that their horizontal competitors were doing the exact same
thing.3 Taking action against one's own independent business interest is a primary "plus factor"
used to infer a collaborative agreement.4 More recently, the Department of Justice (DOJ)
successfully applied this theory in United States v. Apple, proving that Apple acted as the
central hub to orchestrate a conspiracy among major book publishers to raise e-book prices,
utilizing continuous communication and mutual assurances.4
In the contemporary digital era, algorithmic intermediaries frequently serve as the hub. In the
real estate sector, the DOJ and private plaintiffs have pursued aggressive antitrust actions
against RealPage, alleging that competing landlords (the spokes) fed highly confidential pricing
and supply data into a centralized pricing algorithm (the hub) to artificially inflate rental prices
nationwide.3 The legal contention relies heavily on the presence of compliance mechanisms
that pressured users to adhere to the algorithm's outputs, which the DOJ cited as a critical
factor suggesting coordinated, rather than unilateral, behavior.6 The Meyer v. Kalanick case
further cemented this theory, where plaintiffs plausibly alleged that Uber drivers signed up for
the platform on the precise understanding that all other drivers were agreeing to the same
algorithmic pricing structure, creating a hub-and-spoke conspiracy orchestrated by the
algorithm.8
Tacit Collusion and the "Algorithm Made Me Do It" Defense

As AI evolves from traditional rule-based algorithms to autonomous Machine Learning (ML)
and Reinforcement Learning (RL) systems, antitrust law faces a paradigm shift. Empirical
studies demonstrate that LLM-based pricing algorithms and Q-learning systems can
consistently learn to charge supracompetitive prices on their own, without being explicitly
programmed to collude and without direct communication between competitors.3 This
introduces the "Algorithm Made Me Do It" defense, where companies argue that parallel
market behavior is simply the result of autonomous machine optimization rather than an illegal
agreement.3
State lawmakers and federal regulators are increasingly concerned that these tools facilitate
purely tacit collusion, circumventing traditional Section 1 liability which strictly requires proof of
an agreement.10 If competitors independently deploy algorithms that scrape public data and
arrive at uniform market restrictions, courts struggle to find the requisite "meeting of the
minds".11 The legal debate currently centers on whether the mutual delegation of
decision-making to predictable, self-learning algorithms constitutes an implicit agreement to
restrain trade.13
Uniform Throttling of AI Safety Researchers as a Restraint of Trade

The legal theories surrounding algorithmic hub-and-spoke conspiracies and tacit collusion
provide a highly relevant framework for examining the collective behavior of major AI
laboratories (e.g., OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Apple). Independent safety researchers,
alignment auditors, and vulnerability testers heavily rely on consistent, high-volume API access
to conduct their work.15 If these dominant labs uniformly adopt identical, undocumented
"rolling limit" throttling practices and route all researcher account appeals through identical,
automated AI-only support walls, this parallel conduct invites severe antitrust scrutiny under
Section 1.16
A "rolling limit" dynamically restricts API calls based on a hidden, shifting time window, making
systematic algorithmic auditing practically impossible because the researcher cannot replicate
tests consistently or predict their available bandwidth.16 The legal standing for a conspiracy
claim hinges on whether the uniform deployment of these limits across the industry is merely
conscious parallelism or constitutes a coordinated effort to box out independent oversight.
To establish an actionable restraint of trade, regulators must identify the "rim" of the
conspiracy. In the AI ecosystem, a shared cloud infrastructure provider, a common foundational
dataset distributor, or a shared algorithmic security and bot-detection protocol could easily
function as the hub.3 If the AI labs input sensitive usage data into a shared threat-detection
algorithm that uniformly flags and throttles independent safety researchers under the guise of
"abuse prevention" or "spam mitigation," this shared infrastructure acts as the direct conduit for
coordination.8
Furthermore, an inference of a horizontal agreement can be strongly drawn if a lab acts against
its own independent economic interest.4 Rejecting paid API queries from well-funded safety
researchers directly reduces a lab's immediate revenue.7 A lab would logically only incur this
financial loss if it possessed an underlying assurance that its competitors were deploying the
exact same throttling mechanisms, thereby preventing the alienated researcher from simply
migrating to a rival platform to conduct their audits.4 By homogenizing the throttling thresholds
and erecting impenetrable AI-only support walls, the labs effectively establish an industry-wide
boycott of independent algorithmic auditing. This coordinated action shields their proprietary
models from external critique, eliminates the secondary market for independent alignment
verification, and maintains absolute control over the safety narrative.19

2. Monopolization of Frontier Compute under the
Sherman Antitrust Act (Section 2)
While Section 1 addresses collaborative and conspiratorial agreements, Section 2 of the
Sherman Act targets unilateral power, making it illegal to "monopolize, or attempt to
monopolize... any part of the trade or commerce among the several States".20 The offense of
monopolization requires the proof of two distinct elements: first, the possession of monopoly
power in a properly defined relevant market; and second, the willful acquisition, maintenance,
or use of that power for exclusionary purposes, as distinguished from growth resulting from
superior products, business acumen, or historical accident.21
The Shared Monopoly Theory and Market Concentration

The modern AI technology stack is characterized by extreme concentration, specifically at the
foundational hardware and cloud computing layers. Training frontier AI models requires
massive arrays of specialized microprocessors (primarily Nvidia GPUs) and immense
hyperscale cloud computing capacity, markets effectively dominated by Amazon Web Services
(AWS), Google Cloud Platform, and Microsoft Azure.2 The fixed costs associated with model
training are growing exponentially on a logarithmic scale, reaching billions of dollars per training
run, with no current signs of diminishing returns.1 These exorbitant capital requirements and
physical hardware bottlenecks create virtually insurmountable barriers to entry for
independent developers and smaller research laboratories.1
When analyzing this highly concentrated oligopoly, the concept of a "shared monopoly" arises.
A shared monopoly occurs when a small group of dominant firms collectively exercises market
power equivalent to a single monopolist, maintaining supracompetitive prices and foreclosing
entry without necessarily engaging in a formal, prosecutable agreement under Section 1.22
Historically, U.S. federal courts have exhibited profound skepticism toward shared monopoly
claims under Section 2. The First, Second, and other Circuits have consistently rejected the
theory, explicitly holding that Section 2 requires an individual defendant to possess sufficient
market power on its own to constitute a monopoly; the combined power of independent
competitors is legally insufficient for a Section 2 claim.20
However, the traditional rejection of shared monopolies is being challenged by modern
economic frameworks, notably the "Moligopoly" thesis advanced by scholars like Nicolas
Petit.25 This thesis provides a nuanced understanding of digital ecosystems, positing that tech
giants hold deeply entrenched, unassailable monopolies in their core, "tipped" markets (e.g.,
Google in general search, Microsoft in enterprise operating systems, Amazon in e-commerce)
but compete fiercely as an oligopoly in emerging, "untipped" markets like General-Purpose AI
(GPAI).25 Under this framework, the legal vulnerability under Section 2 lies not in a shared
monopoly of AI itself, but in the individual leveraging of core monopoly power to dominate and
foreclose adjacent AI markets.21 For example, the August 2024 landmark ruling by Judge Amit
Mehta in United States v. Google affirmed that Google possessed and abused monopoly
power in the general search market, utilizing exclusionary default agreements to deprive rivals
of data scale.26 The DOJ and private litigants argue that this exact monopolistic power is now
being leveraged to capture the generative AI landscape.21
Exclusionary Conduct and the Suppression of Independent Oversight

The exertion of monopoly power over compute infrastructure directly translates into the ability
to suppress independent oversight. The recent class action complaint in Samuel Bryant et al. v.
Microsoft Corporation (Case No. 3:25-cv-8733, filed October 2025) provides a potent
illustration of Section 2 liability mechanics within the AI sector.28 The complaint alleges that
Microsoft leveraged an exclusive cloud computing agreement with OpenAI to artificially
constrain the supply of compute available to its horizontal competitor.28 By acting as the
exclusive cloud provider, Microsoft reportedly "mercilessly choked" OpenAI's compute supply,
which restricted output, degraded the quality of ChatGPT, and inflated per-token prices by 136
to 200 times the competitive baseline during a 2025 price war initiated by DeepSeek AI.28
When Microsoft partially relaxed this compute restraint in June 2025, OpenAI token prices
immediately plummeted by 80%, clearly demonstrating the market distortion caused by the
exclusionary control of infrastructure.28
This dynamic extends directly to the treatment of independent safety researchers. If a
dominant AI laboratory, backed by an exclusive hyperscale cloud provider, possesses a
chokehold over frontier AI compute, it wields the unilateral capability to revoke access to the
only platforms practically capable of running advanced ethical alignment and safety tests.15
When an AI lab utilizes this monopoly power to arbitrarily throttle a paid, third-party researcher,
it transcends standard network management. It constitutes exclusionary conduct explicitly
designed to foreclose the downstream, secondary market for independent AI auditing and
safety verification.19
By starving independent researchers of the requisite compute and API bandwidth, dominant
labs neutralize nascent competitive threats in the AI safety sector. This ensures that the labs
remain the sole, unquestioned arbiters of what constitutes "safe" or "aligned" AI, thereby
preempting external regulatory intervention, suppressing critical safety narratives, and
maintaining their overarching market dominance.19 The use of monopoly power to deny inputs
to a dependent secondary market is a classic violation of Section 2, particularly when the
refusal to deal lacks a valid, procompetitive business justification.21

3. The "Essential Facilities" Doctrine in the Era of Cloud
Infrastructure and Large Language Models
A highly potent, albeit historically controversial, remedy for unilateral refusals to deal under
Section 2 of the Sherman Act is the "Essential Facilities" doctrine. Originating from early
twentieth-century cases involving physical infrastructure—such as the St. Louis terminal
railroads, where a consortium controlled the only bridges and switching yards into the city—the
doctrine mandates that a monopolist controlling a facility that is absolutely essential to
competition in a downstream market must grant access to competitors on reasonable,
non-discriminatory terms.2
Doctrinal Evolution and the Four-Prong Test

To successfully invoke the Essential Facilities doctrine under established U.S. jurisprudence, a
plaintiff must typically demonstrate four elements, derived from the Seventh Circuit's seminal
ruling in MCI Communications Corp v. AT&T Co.:
   1.​ Control of the essential facility by a monopolist.
   2.​ A competitor's inability practically or reasonably to duplicate the essential facility.
   3.​ The denial of the use of the facility to a competitor.
   4.​ The feasibility of providing the facility to the competitor.2
While the U.S. Supreme Court has expressed skepticism toward the doctrine in recent
decades—most notably in Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko
(2004), where the Court stated that forced sharing could reduce investment incentives and
turn courts into central planners—the doctrine has never been formally repudiated and is
experiencing a renaissance in the context of digital platforms.2 Legal scholars increasingly
argue that digital platforms serve as the railroads of the modern economy, controlling the vital
bottlenecks of commerce and information flow.30
In contrast to the U.S. hesitation, the European Union has actively embraced and expanded
essential facilities-like remedies under Article 102 TFEU. Following cases like Magill, Bronner,
and IMS Health, which required strict indispensability, the European Commission significantly
relaxed the standard in the Microsoft, Google Shopping, and Android Auto cases.25 The EU now
frequently imposes duties to deal and interoperability mandates on digital gatekeepers to
prevent the leveraging of market power into downstream services, prioritizing competition in
the market over the long-term incentives to compete for the market.25
Compute and API Access as Digital Public Utilities

In the contemporary AI ecosystem, hyperscale cloud computing infrastructure (IaaS) and
foundational Large Language Models operate precisely as utility-like bottlenecks.2 Foundation
models and cloud compute infrastructure perfectly map onto the second criterion of the MCI
test: duplication is economically and practically infeasible for independent researchers.1 The
multi-billion-dollar fixed costs of acquiring vast GPU clusters, securing massive proprietary
training datasets, and paying elite engineering talent place the creation of frontier models
entirely out of reach for academic institutions, non-profits, and independent auditing startups.1
Consequently, access to the APIs of these dominant models is an absolute prerequisite for
participating in the downstream market of AI application development and safety verification.15
The Denial of Essential Facilities to Paid Researchers

The application of this doctrine is actively playing out in the AI market, highlighting the severe
risks of unregulated gatekeeping. A critical case study is Anthropic's 2025 termination of API
access for Windsurf, a startup that developed a highly successful AI coding agent.15 Anthropic
deliberately severed Windsurf's access to the Claude API upon rumors that OpenAI might
acquire the startup.15 Anthropic's executives explicitly cited competitive motivations, seeking to
avoid empowering a primary rival, while simultaneously protecting their own vertically
integrated coding agent, Claude Code.15 Similarly, Anthropic restricted xAI's use of Cursor,
demonstrating a willingness to use the API as a "kill switch" to execute a discriminatory refusal
to deal against a "customer's customer".15
When independent safety researchers are denied access or arbitrarily throttled without an
appeal mechanism, they suffer a similar anticompetitive foreclosure. Safety researchers
operate in a secondary, downstream market: algorithmic auditing, bias detection, and
compliance verification. If they cannot conduct their research without access to the
proprietary weights, fine-tuning mechanisms, and high-volume APIs of the frontier models, the
LLM itself unequivocally becomes an essential facility.25
The arbitrary, unappealable throttling of a paid safety researcher therefore constitutes an illegal
denial of an essential facility, provided it can be proven that the AI lab wields monopoly power
and that providing access is technically feasible.2 Given that the labs already provide
commercial API access at scale, allocating un-throttled bandwidth to a paying researcher is
highly feasible and poses no technical burden.2 To combat these abuses, scholars and
policymakers are increasingly advocating for an "AI Neutrality" mandate—akin to net neutrality
rules for telecommunications—that would legally prohibit foundation model providers from
unjustly discriminating, throttling, or artificially altering the latency and quality of service for
third-party downstream users and auditors.15

4. Regulatory Oversight: The FTC Act (Section 5) and
Unfair Methods of Competition
Beyond the strict and heavily litigated strictures of the Sherman and Clayton Acts, federal
regulators possess broader, more flexible authority under Section 5 of the Federal Trade
Commission (FTC) Act. Section 5 explicitly prohibits "unfair methods of competition in or
affecting commerce" alongside "unfair or deceptive acts or practices".36 This statutory authority
is deliberately designed to arrest anticompetitive behavior in its incipiency, granting the FTC the
mandate to target conduct that violates the spirit of antitrust laws, even if it does not
technically fulfill the rigid, judicially narrowed requirements of Section 1 or Section 2 of the
Sherman Act.22
Current Agency Inquiries into Big Tech AI Monopolies

The FTC, under the leadership of Chair Lina M. Khan, has aggressively utilized its Section 5
enforcement authority and its Section 6(b) investigatory powers to scrutinize the highly
concentrated AI landscape. Section 6(b) authorizes the Commission to conduct wide-ranging,
mandatory studies that do not require a specific law enforcement purpose, compelling
companies to submit detailed internal documents regarding their business practices.37
In early 2024, the FTC launched comprehensive 6(b) inquiries into the multi-billion-dollar
investments and partnerships between the dominant cloud service providers (CSPs) and
leading AI developers. Specifically, the orders targeted Microsoft's relationship with OpenAI,
Amazon's partnership with Anthropic, and Google's partnership with Anthropic.39 The resulting
2025 FTC staff report revealed profound concerns regarding these "co-opetition" dynamics.1
The report highlighted that these partnerships often grant the CSPs extensive equity,
revenue-sharing rights, and exclusive access to sensitive technical information.40 This structure
effectively creates "lock-in," deprives nascent startups of key AI inputs (such as engineering
talent and compute resources), and reveals sensitive data that can systematically undermine
fair competition.40
Through these complex investments, dominant tech firms create "walled gardens," utilizing
restrictive Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) to exert total control over the
downstream data flow and application layer.22 The FTC has historically viewed the strategic
closing of APIs to stifle dependent third parties as an unfair method of competition. Notable
precedents include the FTC's Section 5 actions against Intel for redesigning software to
purposefully disadvantage competing CPUs, and actions against Dell for reneging on
information-sharing agreements essential for industry interoperability.22 If major AI labs utilize
their market dominance to arbitrarily throttle independent safety researchers, this behavior
squarely aligns with the FTC's definition of an incipient tying arrangement or a breach of
interoperability designed to "pick winners" and insulate the dominant platform from external
scrutiny.22
Mechanisms for Filing Formal Complaints Regarding Algorithmic
Throttling

For private citizens, independent labs, or safety researchers facing algorithmic throttling
explicitly designed to suppress safety narratives, the federal government provides specific
avenues for regulatory redress. While the FTC does not generally resolve individual private
contractual disputes, it actively aggregates complaints to identify patterns of systemic
anticompetitive behavior that warrant federal intervention and broad enforcement actions.42
An independent researcher experiencing unappealable API revocation, discriminatory
throttling, or the imposition of exclusionary digital barriers can file a formal complaint directly
with the FTC's Bureau of Competition via their dedicated antitrust complaint intake
webform.42 To maximize the efficacy of such a complaint, the researcher must frame the
throttling not merely as a breach of terms of service or poor customer support, but
strategically as an "unfair method of competition" under Section 5 of the FTC Act. The
complaint must meticulously document how the AI lab is leveraging its gatekeeping power to
foreclose the secondary market of AI safety auditing. By doing so, the lab maintains proprietary
control over public safety narratives and protects its core monopoly from regulatory or
competitive disruption generated by negative audit findings.19
Concurrently, if the independent safety research is funded by government grants or involves
federal procurement, complaints concerning anticompetitive impacts can be routed to the
DOJ's Procurement Collusion Strike Force (PCSF).44 Furthermore, researchers employed by
these labs or affiliated entities who blow the whistle on these practices are protected from
employer retaliation under the Criminal Antitrust Anti-Retaliation Act of 2019 (CAARA), and can
file retaliation complaints directly with the Occupational Safety and Health Administration
(OSHA).45 By utilizing these formal mechanisms, researchers can trigger the FTC's immense
investigative powers, including the issuance of Civil Investigative Demands (CIDs) and
subpoenas under Section 9 of the FTC Act, to compel the disclosure of the proprietary
algorithms used to enact the throttling.37

5. Discriminatory Monopolies: The Intersection of
Antitrust Law and the ADA
The most novel, legally complex, and arguably insidious dimension of AI gatekeeping lies at the
intersection of antitrust law and civil rights—specifically, Title III of the Americans with
Disabilities Act (ADA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. When a monopoly or a highly
concentrated cartel possesses the absolute market power to dictate the technological
standards of an entire industry, the adoption of exclusionary digital infrastructure transcends
mere poor user experience. It becomes a systemic, structural barrier to entry that
disproportionately and illegally affects protected classes, weaponizing digital exclusion as an
anticompetitive tool.47
Digital Infrastructure Standards and Statutory Precedent

Title III of the ADA mandates that places of "public accommodation" ensure effective
communication and equal access to goods and services for individuals with disabilities.49
Historically, federal circuit courts have been split on whether online-only businesses lacking a
physical brick-and-mortar storefront constitute public accommodations.50 However, recent
jurisprudence and sweeping regulatory updates by the DOJ have increasingly affirmed that
digital platforms, APIs, and mobile applications must conform to strict accessibility standards,
most notably the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA.49
In April 2024, the DOJ published a final rule under Title II of the ADA mandating WCAG 2.1 AA
compliance for state and local government digital properties.52 Legal experts widely agree this
Title II rule establishes the de facto standard that will soon be aggressively applied to private
sector Title III entities.47 Furthermore, in May 2024, the Department of Health and Human
Services (HHS) finalized a historic rule under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, explicitly
requiring that all web content and mobile apps developed by organizations receiving federal
funding conform to WCAG 2.1 AA.53 Section 504 explicitly treats inaccessible digital
experiences as actionable disability discrimination.55 Together, these precedents establish a
clear federal mandate: digital infrastructure must be accessible. The failure to comply has
resulted in a massive surge in litigation, with plaintiffs filing 4,187 digital accessibility lawsuits in
2024 alone.48 Key cases, such as Robles v. Domino's and Gil v. Winn-Dixie, have cemented the
legal reality that digital inaccessibility yields immense liability.57
Exclusionary Design as an Anticompetitive Moat

In the context of frontier AI, major laboratories often deploy highly automated, AI-driven
support walls to handle API quota requests, account appeals, and throttling inquiries.16 These
automated systems are frequently designed without comprehensive accessibility overlays,
utilizing complex visual CAPTCHAs, poorly coded dynamic web elements, or strictly
mouse-dependent interfaces that are entirely incompatible with screen readers, voice
navigation, or other assistive technologies relied upon by disabled researchers.58
When a competitive market functions properly, a disabled user facing an inaccessible interface
can simply take their business to a competitor offering an accessible alternative. However, in a
monopolistic or perfectly cartelized market, there are no viable alternatives.26 If the GAMMANs
universally adopt inaccessible AI support walls, they effectively establish an industry-wide
standard that systematically excludes disabled populations from participating in the AI
economy, without offering any alternatives.26
This digital exclusion serves a profound, deeply anticompetitive purpose. The sociological and
legal theory regarding the "aesthetics of disability" posits that structural discrimination often
relies on minimizing contact between disabled individuals and the dominant economic
infrastructure.60 By deploying inaccessible digital choke-points, AI monopolies functionally
sanitize their user base, filtering out individuals who require customized, high-touch support or
who might deploy specialized accessibility scripts that algorithms misidentify as "high-volume"
or "bot" API usage.
When an independent safety researcher with a visual or motor disability is throttled by an AI
lab's opaque "rolling limit" and subsequently trapped behind an inaccessible, automated appeal
system, the injury is twofold. First, the researcher is denied the essential facility of the LLM,
entirely halting their vital auditing work.25 Second, and more critically from an antitrust
perspective, the monopolistic firm insulates itself from the legal, competitive, and public
relations pressures that a diverse, independent auditing body would inevitably generate. By
passively enforcing a digital standard that violates WCAG 2.1 AA and ADA Title III, the AI cartel
homogenizes the pool of actors capable of interacting with its systems, locking out disabled
researchers who might uncover novel algorithmic biases or safety vulnerabilities.48
This intersection creates a compelling, multi-front legal vulnerability for Big Tech. Antitrust
regulators (DOJ/FTC) and civil rights litigators can converge on the premise that the failure to
provide accessible alternatives is not merely a technical oversight or a basic ADA violation, but
a calculated, exclusionary standard-setting practice. When this failure is engineered by a
monopoly to lock out a specific subset of researchers, it bridges the gap between civil rights
violations and exclusionary anticompetitive conduct under Section 2 of the Sherman Act,
demonstrating how market power can be weaponized to evade both accessibility mandates
and independent safety oversight.

6. Comprehensive Synthesis and Strategic Outlook
The rapid consolidation of the artificial intelligence sector, driven by insurmountable capital
requirements and massive compute bottlenecks, demands a robust and multifaceted
regulatory response. As this exhaustive analysis demonstrates, the collective behavior of major
AI laboratories—specifically the arbitrary throttling of API access, the deployment of
unappealable automated support walls, and the leveraging of exclusive hyperscale
computational advantages—violates the foundational principles of competition law across
multiple statutory dimensions.
   1.​ Collusion and Coordination: Under Section 1 of the Sherman Act, the uniform,
       industry-wide adoption of restrictive API limits and automated blockades can be
       aggressively prosecuted as a hub-and-spoke conspiracy. This requires demonstrating
       that labs rely on shared algorithmic infrastructure or act against their independent
       economic interests to maintain a collective boycott of independent safety researchers,
       thereby chilling the secondary market for alignment auditing.3
   2.​ Monopolization of Compute: Under Section 2 of the Sherman Act, the absolute control
       over hyperscale compute and foundational models by a highly concentrated oligopoly
       constitutes an actionable shared monopoly under modern frameworks like the
       Moligopoly thesis.1 The weaponization of this power to choke compute supply and
       degrade downstream applications—as explicitly alleged in the recent Bryant v. Microsoft
       litigation—illustrates a clear pattern of exclusionary conduct intended to neutralize
       competitive threats and suppress oversight.28
   3.​ Essential Facilities: Because the multi-billion-dollar cost of training frontier models
       renders duplication practically impossible, LLMs and their underlying cloud infrastructure
       act as modern essential facilities. The discriminatory revocation of access, exemplified by
       the 2025 Anthropic/Windsurf incident, establishes a dangerous precedent of dominant
       firms starving downstream competitors and safety auditors of essential digital public
       utilities.2
   4.​ FTC Enforcement: The FTC's broad authority under Section 5 of the FTC Act perfectly
       encapsulates the incipient threat of AI "walled gardens" and the lock-in effects of CSP
       partnerships. Formal complaints to the Bureau of Competition regarding unfair
       algorithmic throttling offer a direct mechanism for researchers to challenge the labs'
       efforts to exercise proprietary control over the public safety narrative.22
   5.​ Civil Rights Intersection: The most profound regulatory insight emerges from the
       synthesis of antitrust principles and the ADA. By embedding inaccessible digital
       standards into the very support walls required to appeal API throttling, monopolies
       systematically exclude disabled researchers.48 This conduct transcends traditional
       discrimination; it functions as a calculated anticompetitive moat that actively insulates
       dominant models from rigorous, diverse, and independent algorithmic auditing, shielding
       the gatekeepers from accountability.
To preserve the integrity of the technological frontier and ensure that the trajectory of
General-Purpose AI is subjected to independent ethical alignment, regulatory bodies must look
beyond traditional price-theory antitrust enforcement. They must embrace the deeply
interconnected nature of digital civil rights, essential facilities mandates, and algorithmic
collusion to systematically dismantle the gatekeeping mechanisms currently insulating the
world's most powerful AI laboratories.
