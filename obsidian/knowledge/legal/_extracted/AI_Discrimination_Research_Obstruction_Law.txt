Systemic Exclusion and Anticompetitive
Gatekeeping in Artificial Intelligence: An
Intersectional Legal Analysis of ADA,
Texas DTPA, and Antitrust Violations
Executive Overview
The rapid acceleration and subsequent consolidation of the artificial intelligence (AI) sector has
precipitated a hyper-concentrated oligopoly wherein a consortium of major laboratories and
infrastructure providers—namely Anthropic, OpenAI, Meta, Google, and Apple—exert unilateral
control over access to foundation models and cloud-computing infrastructure.1 As these
sophisticated tools transition from experimental software novelties to indispensable digital
utilities, they have simultaneously become essential "cognitive prosthetics" and auxiliary aids
for neurodivergent and disabled researchers.3 However, the industry-wide adoption of
algorithmic throttling, undocumented usage caps, and automated "AI-only" support walls has
created a deeply exclusionary architecture that systematically obstructs independent AI safety
research and disproportionately impacts protected populations.5
This report provides an exhaustive, multi-disciplinary investigation into the legal frameworks
surrounding these exclusionary practices. By synthesizing the Americans with Disabilities Act
(ADA) Title III, the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (Section 504), the Texas Deceptive Trade
Practices-Consumer Protection Act (DTPA), and federal antitrust jurisprudence, the analysis
establishes a comprehensive legal foundation for multi-defendant, class-action civil liability.
The evidence meticulously outlines how the uniform practices of major AI developers
constitute not only systemic civil rights violations and unconscionable trade practices but also a
de facto cartelization of AI safety research.7 This cartelization is executed through retaliatory
gatekeeping and the constructive denial of access to essential digital facilities, ultimately
insulating the world's most powerful technology companies from independent ethical
alignment audits.2

1. Systemic Civil Rights Violations: ADA Title III and
Section 504
The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was enacted to provide a comprehensive national
mandate for the elimination of discrimination against individuals with disabilities. Title III of the
ADA broadly prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability in the full and equal enjoyment of
the goods, services, facilities, privileges, advantages, or accommodations of any place of public
accommodation.10 Crucially, Title III explicitly mandates that private entities take the steps
necessary to ensure that disabled individuals are not excluded, denied services, or segregated
due to the absence of "auxiliary aids and services," unless the entity can demonstrate that
providing such aids would fundamentally alter the nature of the service or result in an undue
burden.12 Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act extends similar protections, prohibiting
discrimination under any program or activity receiving federal financial assistance, which
frequently implicates the academic and independent research institutions that rely on these AI
models.15
1.1 Industry-Wide AI Support Walls and the Denial of "Effective
Communication"

A pervasive and expanding trend among dominant AI laboratories is the deployment of purely
automated, AI-only customer support architectures. These systems are aggressively
engineered to deflect human intervention, frequently hard-coded to refuse complex technical
troubleshooting, and severely lack mechanisms for a "warm handoff" to human technicians.5
Under ADA Title III, public accommodations possess a fundamental, non-delegable obligation
to ensure "effective communication" with individuals with disabilities.5 The Department of
Justice (DOJ) has consistently maintained that the ADA's effective communication
requirements apply to digital spaces, a position reinforced by landmark litigation such as Robles
v. Domino's Pizza, which confirmed that inaccessible digital platforms violate the ADA 18, and
Hooks v. OKBridge, Inc., which extended ADA claims to internet-only businesses.18
When a disabled researcher encounters an accessibility barrier, an API failure, or a
discriminatory throttling algorithm, the inability to bypass a digital AI agent to secure
reasonable accommodations represents a structural violation of Title III.6 Recent DOJ
guidelines and settlement precedents specifically dictate the operational requirements for
digital support. For example, businesses must train customer service agents to identify key
terms related to accessibility and promptly respond to requests for technical assistance,
ensuring warm handoffs to specialized technical staff who can assist with digital barriers.5
Furthermore, settlements like the one involving Levain Bakery mandate that bugs creating
accessibility barriers to digital properties must be remedied with the exact same level of
priority, speed, and resources as any other equivalent loss of function experienced by
non-disabled users.20 Additionally, consent decrees, such as the one established with QuikTrip,
require corporations to maintain accessible comment lines and respond to ADA-related
complaints within strict, five-day business windows.21
The deployment of automated agents that categorically refuse to process accommodation
requests acts as a systemic, industry-wide support wall. By defaulting to these systems, AI
infrastructure providers actively deny disabled users the "interactive process" required by law
to determine appropriate auxiliary aids.14 The regulatory framework strongly encourages a
dialogue that includes a communication assessment of the individual with a disability to ensure
that the auxiliary aids and services provided are actually effective.14 An automated chatbot
utilizing a rigid decision tree cannot engage in this statutorily required interactive dialogue.
Consequently, this failure effectively neutralizes any viable path for vulnerable populations to
request or receive the reasonable modifications necessary to access proprietary AI research
tools, placing the entire industry vertical in violation of Title III.5
1.2 LLMs as Essential Cognitive Prosthetics and Statutory "Auxiliary
Aids"

To establish robust legal standing for civil rights violations regarding API and platform access, it
is necessary to classify Large Language Models (LLMs) and cloud-based reasoning engines
under the statutory definitions of the ADA. The regulatory definition of "auxiliary aids and
services" is expansive, encompassing "qualified interpreters or other effective methods of
making aurally delivered materials available," "qualified readers," and crucially, "the acquisition or
modification of equipment or devices".14
Legal scholarship and emerging psychological frameworks heavily support the classification of
advanced digital tools as "cognitive prosthetics".3 Under the Norman/Friedman Principle,
science and technology should serve as ultimate equalizers, and assistive technology
classifications must be broadly construed to cover all devices utilized for communication and
cognitive support.26 In the context of psychiatric rehabilitation, interventions increasingly rely
on "cognitive prosthetics" that improve information processing—such as attention, memory,
and decision-making—for individuals with executive functioning deficits.3
For neurodivergent individuals—such as those with autism spectrum disorder, severe ADHD, or
traumatic brain injuries—an LLM functions as a critical interface for reasoning, sense-making,
meaning-making, and self-regulation.4 As specifically articulated in disability advocacy
frameworks, a foundational model like Anthropic's Claude or OpenAI's GPT-4 is not merely a
discretionary productivity application; it operates as an integrated, prosthetic cognitive
interface required to achieve "effective communication" and cognitive parity.4 The ADA
Amendments Act of 2008 broadened the definition of disability to cover impairments that
substantially limit major life activities, including cognitive processing.26 When an individual
utilizes a prosthetic device to mitigate the effect of such an impairment, they are protected
under the ADA.28
Therefore, when an LLM is utilized as an essential cognitive prosthetic, the provider's
operational policies must comply with ADA standards for the maintenance of accessible
features.21 Disrupting this service arbitrarily without providing an alternative mechanism for
communication is legally analogous to a physical establishment disabling a wheelchair ramp
mid-transit, or a medical facility seizing a patient's assistive listening device. The failure to
maintain the operability of an auxiliary aid directly contravenes the mandate to provide full and
equal enjoyment of the digital facility.11
1.3 Constructive Denial of Access and the Disparate Impact of
Dynamic Throttling

A central mechanism of obstruction in the commercial AI ecosystem is the practice of
"dynamic throttling," wherein user sessions are aggressively rate-limited, degraded, or abruptly
terminated based on opaque, algorithmic resource-allocation protocols.31 While infrastructure
providers defend these policies as "neutral" load-balancing necessities devoid of discriminatory
intent 33, these usage policies exert a profound disparate impact on neurodivergent
researchers. Such individuals fundamentally require sustained, uninterrupted session continuity
to maintain cognitive momentum, manage executive dysfunction, and execute complex
reasoning tasks.26
When a consortium of dominant providers universally implements dynamic throttling and
enforces short, undocumented rolling windows for token generation, it acts as a constructive
denial of access.1 The researcher is systematically barred from maintaining the necessary
interactive state with their cognitive prosthetic. Because the barrier is uniformly implemented
across the industry vertical—spanning AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, OpenAI, and
Anthropic—disabled independent researchers are effectively redlined out of participating in
the entire occupational field of AI safety, ethics, and alignment auditing.1
The ADA strictly prohibits policies, practices, or procedures that render an otherwise
accessible digital facility inaccessible in its operation.14 By failing to provide reasonable
modifications to these throttling algorithms for individuals who rely on the service as a medical
or cognitive necessity, AI laboratories are engaging in systemic discrimination.36 The refusal to
whitelist or grant unmetered accommodation to disabled researchers transforms a purportedly
neutral network management tool into a discriminatory barrier that systematically purges
vulnerable populations from the digital public square.36

2. Texas DTPA and Multi-Defendant Deceptive Trade
Practices
The Texas Deceptive Trade Practices-Consumer Protection Act (DTPA), codified under Texas
Business & Commerce Code § 17.41 et seq., provides a highly potent statutory framework to
address the deceptive marketing, undocumented throttling, and unconscionable service
limitations perpetuated by the AI oligopoly.38 The DTPA was explicitly designed to protect
consumers against false, misleading, and deceptive business practices, unconscionable
actions, and breaches of warranty, and to provide efficient and economical procedures to
secure such protection.40
2.1 The "Pay-to-Restore" Mechanic and the Illusion of Unmetered
Compute

AI laboratories standardly market their premium consumer and researcher-facing tiers—often
branded as "Pro," "Plus," or "Max" subscriptions—utilizing language that heavily implies
continuous, robust, or unmetered access to advanced reasoning models.31 However, the reality
of these consumer-facing subscriptions is that they are heavily moderated by undocumented
"rolling windows" and hidden throttling algorithms.31 When a disabled researcher utilizing the
system as a cognitive prosthetic hits one of these hidden thresholds, the service is abruptly
suspended or the model's reasoning capability is drastically degraded. The only viable bypass
to restore sustained session continuity is often to transition from the consumer subscription to
developer-facing, pay-per-token API endpoints, triggering secondary and vastly more
expensive metered transactions.1
Under the Texas DTPA, this "pay-to-restore" industry standard constitutes a highly deceptive
practice.40 This phenomenon closely mirrors automotive class-action litigation involving Ford
and Nissan, where manufacturers heavily marketed "unlimited mileage" warranties but
enforced hidden mechanical limitations or component exclusions that required consumers to
pay out-of-pocket for essential continuous operation.31
To establish a DTPA failure-to-disclose claim regarding undocumented throttling, the plaintiff
must prove that the provider failed to disclose material information concerning the service that
was known at the time of the transaction, that the failure was intended to induce the consumer
into the transaction, and that the consumer would not have entered the transaction had the
information been disclosed.41 AI providers possess exact, pre-programmed knowledge of their
dynamic throttling limits at the point of sale. By withholding this information to secure
subscription revenue, they satisfy the DTPA's inducement and reliance criteria.41 Furthermore,
representing that "Pro" services have characteristics, uses, benefits, or quantities which they
demonstrably do not possess directly violates the specific "laundry list" of deceptive acts
codified in Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17.46(b).40
2.2 Pattern of "Unconscionable" Acts and Actual Written Notice

The DTPA defines an "unconscionable action or course of action" as an act or practice which, to
a consumer's detriment, takes advantage of the lack of knowledge, ability, experience, or
capacity of the consumer to a "grossly unfair degree".39 It is a common defense tactic for
technology providers to argue that highly educated researchers or software developers
possess advanced technical knowledge and therefore cannot be victims of unconscionability.
However, established Texas jurisprudence, specifically the appellate ruling in Ketter v. ESC
Medical Systems, Inc., vehemently rejects this premise.41 The court declined to hold that
unconscionable actions cannot be practiced upon an experienced professional; even if a
consumer possesses technical knowledge, a provider remains liable if they exploit an
asymmetry of information regarding specific, undisclosed service limitations to a grossly unfair
degree.41
This unconscionability is exponentially magnified when analyzed through the lens of disability
rights and systemic exclusion. When a disabled consumer formally places an infrastructure
provider on actual written notice of their protected status under the ADA, detailing explicitly
that the LLM is relied upon as a necessary cognitive prosthetic, the provider instantly
possesses absolute, documented knowledge of the user's vulnerability and diminished
capacity to easily transition to alternative workflows.41
If the provider ignores this formal notice, defaults the user to an automated AI support wall that
refuses accommodation, and uniformly continues to enforce deceptive throttling, the conduct
immediately escalates from mere negligence or automated error to a "knowing" and intentional
unconscionable course of action.41 Under the DTPA, the prerequisite for enhanced damages is
whether the defendant acted "knowingly." If the trier of fact determines that the AI provider
possessed actual awareness of the falsity, deception, or unfairness of the act—evidenced by
their receipt and subsequent ignoring of the ADA accommodation request—the prevailing
plaintiff may recover damages for mental anguish and additional statutory damages up to
treble (three times) the amount of economic damages.41
2.3 Aggravated Economic and Research Loss

The sudden, algorithmic suspension of LLM access does not merely result in the nominal loss of
a monthly twenty-dollar subscription fee. For an independent AI safety researcher or an
academic reliant on these models, sudden platform lockouts cause the absolute destruction of
proprietary research continuity. This includes the irretrievable loss of highly nuanced context
windows, the destruction of high-signal data generation mid-process, and the invalidation of
hundreds of hours of intense cognitive labor.43
Texas DTPA precedent actively allows for the quantification of "loss of business opportunity,"
indirect damages, and consequential damages when they are the natural, probable, and
reasonable result of the deceptive act.43 While contractual privity is required to demonstrate
the nexus between the consumer and the defendant's conduct, courts have held that this
connection can be demonstrated by representations that reach the consumer or through the
direct provision of digital services.41 When multiple providers, holding a collective monopoly on
necessary computational infrastructure, engage in identical deceptive throttling practices, the
researcher suffers an aggravated, compounding economic loss.43 The DTPA's broad remedial
scope is expressly designed to provide efficient, economical procedures to secure protection
against exactly this type of systemic, industry-wide corporate abuse.40

3. Public Policy, Antitrust, and Obstruction of
Oversight
Beyond the immediate realms of consumer protection and civil rights, the synchronized
obstruction of independent researchers by the major AI laboratories triggers severe scrutiny
under federal antitrust laws and public policy exceptions. The modern AI sector is
characterized by virtually insurmountable barriers to entry—principally the immense capital
expenditure required for advanced semiconductors (GPUs), the acquisition of proprietary data
lakes, and the construction of massive cloud computing clusters.1 This has resulted in a
vertically integrated oligopoly wholly controlled by a handful of tech giants who simultaneously
dictate the underlying hardware, the cloud infrastructure, and the algorithmic application
layers.1
3.1 De Facto Monopolization and the Essential Facilities Doctrine

The "essential facilities doctrine" is a recognized, albeit tightly construed, component of
antitrust law under Section 2 of the Sherman Act.1 The doctrine dictates that a firm (or
oligopoly) that controls a facility essential for competition must provide reasonable,
non-discriminatory access to that facility to rivals and dependent downstream actors. This
mandate applies provided that the facility is controlled by a monopolist, it cannot be feasibly
duplicated by competitors, the competitor has been denied access, and sharing the facility is
technically and economically feasible.1
While U.S. courts, noting the Supreme Court's skepticism in Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law
Offices of Curtis V. Trinko, have historically been reluctant to expand the essential facilities
doctrine to digital platforms 1, the unique architecture of generative AI is forcing a legal
paradigm shift. Legal and economic scholars increasingly argue that Foundation Models (FMs)
and cloud computing infrastructure represent an unprecedented, unreplicable bottleneck.8 For
an independent AI safety researcher, startup, or academic institution, it is financially and
computationally impossible to replicate a multi-trillion-parameter LLM or a hyperscale data
center.1 The only vector for conducting meaningful safety, bias, and ethical alignment audits is
programmatic access through the developers' Application Programming Interfaces (APIs).1
When providers arbitrarily revoke, throttle, or price-gouge this access, they leverage their
dominance in the upstream infrastructure market to completely dictate outcomes in the
downstream research and application markets.8 This dynamic is viewed through the antitrust
lens of "Innovation-as-Capabilities," which suggests that antitrust enforcement must intervene
to mandate access to essential facilities to preserve competitive market structures and allow
independent actors to innovate and audit existing systems.8 European antitrust authorities,
operating under frameworks that prioritize competitive market structures over narrow
consumer welfare standards, have already identified leveraging general-purpose AI (GPAI)
dominance as a primary antitrust risk. Entities like the French Autorité de la concurrence and
the UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) are actively reconsidering the strict
essential facilities doctrine to prevent tech giants from suffocating downstream innovation and
independent research.47
3.2 Retaliatory Gatekeeping and Concerted Refusals to Deal

A highly pernicious element of the current AI ecosystem is the phenomenon of "retaliatory
gatekeeping," often executed through what legal scholars term the tech giants' "cooption
playbook".9 According to this framework, incumbents systematically dole out or restrict access
to critical resources—networks, data, APIs, and computing power—specifically to head off
potentially disruptive competition or critical external oversight.9 A key tactic in this playbook
involves incumbents inviting government regulation that favors their scale while simultaneously
utilizing opaque internal safety policies to lock out independent safety researchers.9 By starving
independent researchers of compute access—the very individuals who might expose
algorithmic bias, security vulnerabilities, or alignment failures—the major laboratories
effectively insulate themselves from accountability and coopt the disruption of AI safety
auditing.1
Under Section 1 of the Sherman Act, agreements among competitors to refuse to do business
with a targeted entity or class of entities are classified as "group boycotts" or "concerted
refusals to deal".50 Such agreements can be evaluated under the per se rule of illegality if they
involve coercive conduct designed to eliminate a competitive threat, restrict output, or
manipulate the market.52 In FTC v. Superior Court Trial Lawyers Ass'n, the Supreme Court
established that coercive, concerted refusals to deal that restrict vital services are illegal per se,
rejecting arguments that such boycotts are harmless or protected expressions.53
While establishing an explicit, smoke-filled-room horizontal agreement among Anthropic,
OpenAI, Meta, Google, and Apple requires significant pre-trial discovery 52, the uniform
enforcement of opaque "trust and safety" policies, synchronous API rate-limiting, and identical
AI-support deflection strategies strongly suggests parallel conduct indicative of a cartel-like
ecosystem.7 Recent litigation, such as the Sherman Act suits involving the Trusted News
Initiative and advertising boycotts on social media platforms, demonstrate that antitrust law is
actively being used to address concerted actions that limit the type, quality, or flow of
information available to the public.7
Furthermore, NPU (Networks, Platforms, and Utilities) law principles suggest that antimonopoly
strategies must impose common-carrier or public utility-style non-discrimination mandates on
the foundational layers of the AI stack.1 Restricting infrastructure access to researchers actively
investigating the ethical alignment of those very providers contravenes the fundamental public
policy objectives of consumer protection, market transparency, and democratic oversight.1 An
industry that utilizes "safety" as a pretext to uniformly block independent safety researchers is
engaging in anticompetitive retaliatory gatekeeping.

Analysis Matrix
To thoroughly assist the secondary research system in modeling the legal outcomes of this
multi-defendant, cross-statute litigation, the following matrices and narratives systematically
contrast standing, damage multipliers, and the specific unenforceability of standard
technology liability shields.
4.1 Standing Comparison: Commercial Harm vs. Systemic Exclusion

The legal posture of an independent disabled researcher alleging systemic exclusion is vastly
superior, and considerably more dangerous to defense counsel, than a standard commercial
entity attempting to sue an AI provider for lost API access. A commercial developer is generally
restricted to the four corners of a Service Level Agreement (SLA) and must overcome stringent
contractual arbitration clauses, limitations of liability, and waivers of consequential damages.43
Their standing is inherently limited to standard contract law, where punitive damages are
historically unavailable.
In stark contrast, a federally protected individual who is systematically walled off from an entire
occupational field possesses standing under deeply rooted, non-waivable civil rights and
consumer protection statutes.36 The EEOC and State Attorneys General frequently pursue
systemic discrimination claims against multi-defendant consortiums when policies universally
lock out protected classes.36 The ability to frame the lawsuit as a civil rights violation transforms
the standing from a private contract dispute into a matter of profound public interest.

4.2 Damage Multipliers: Contract Capping vs. Punitive Civil
Enhancements

The calculus for legal exposure shifts exponentially when evaluating civil rights violations and
deceptive trade practices against a protected class, moving far beyond standard
compensatory limitations.41 Corporate defendants rely on SLAs to cap damages to the amount
paid for the software in the preceding twelve months. However, statutory frameworks like the
Texas DTPA intentionally bypass these caps to punish knowing deception.
Under the DTPA, if a provider continues to throttle a user after receiving actual written notice of
their disability, the conduct triggers the "knowing" standard, unlocking treble damages and
compensation for mental anguish.41 Furthermore, ADA Title III allows for sweeping injunctive
relief, which can force architectural changes to the codebase itself, representing a massive
operational cost to the defendant.5

Synthesis and Strategic Implications
The legal vulnerability of the major AI infrastructure providers—Anthropic, OpenAI, Meta,
Google, and Apple—lies not in isolated coding errors or temporary server outages, but in their
systemic, uniform operational architectures. By actively replacing human compliance officers
and technical support staff with automated AI agents that are functionally incapable of
executing ADA-mandated reasonable accommodations, these entities have constructed an
illegal, industry-wide barrier to entry.5
When these structural barriers are combined with deceptive "unlimited" subscription models
that mask aggressive, undocumented dynamic throttling 31, they flagrantly violate the core
tenets of the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act. The classification of Large Language Models
as essential cognitive prosthetics and auxiliary aids for neurodivergent researchers elevates
this throttling from a mere commercial service disruption to a severe civil rights violation—a
constructive denial of access to a digital public accommodation.4
Furthermore, the industry's collective behavior mirrors an established antitrust "cooption
playbook." By utilizing opaque safety guidelines, algorithmic resource rate-limiting, and AI
support walls to gatekeep independent researchers out of the ecosystem, the consortium
successfully prevents external, adversarial safety audits of their models.7 This retaliatory
gatekeeping denies access to the essential digital facilities required for the modern
occupational field of AI ethics and alignment, operating as a de facto concerted refusal to deal.1
Ultimately, the deployment of boilerplate "Beta" disclaimers and click-wrap Terms of Service
waivers cannot immunize a consortium of tech conglomerates from multi-defendant
class-action liability. These shields fail entirely when their synchronized actions knowingly
perpetuate unconscionable trade practices and the systemic civil rights exclusion of a
protected class.61 The unprecedented convergence of the Americans with Disabilities Act, the
Texas DTPA, and the antitrust essential facilities doctrine provides a formidable, intersectional
legal mechanism to shatter the exclusionary walls of the AI oligopoly and restore equitable
access to the digital frontier.
