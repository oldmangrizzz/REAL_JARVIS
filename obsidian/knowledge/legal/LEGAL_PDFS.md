## 1. AI Antitrust and Essential Facilities

- **File:** `CANON/corpus/AI_Antitrust_Essential_Facilities.pdf` (380 KB)
- **Extract:** `legal/_extracted/AI_Antitrust_Essential_Facilities.txt` (725 lines)
- **Thesis:** The "GAMMANs" (Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Apple, Nvidia) form a hyper-concentrated oligopoly over AI infrastructure (compute, model, distribution layers). Uniform "rolling limits," unappealable automated support walls, and discriminatory API revocations constitute (a) §1 Sherman Act algorithmic collusion, (b) §2 Sherman Act monopolization via the Essential Facilities doctrine, (c) §5 FTC Act "unfair methods," and (d) novel ADA/§504 intersections when the victims are disabled safety researchers.
- **Key doctrines:** Sherman §1 & §2; Essential Facilities (*MCI v. AT&T*, *Trinko* limits); FTC §5; civil-rights intersection.
- **Relevance to JARVIS:** Grounds the TinCan Firewall's antitrust arm — the reason JARVIS must run on sovereign infra and not be contingent on any vendor API.

## 2. AI Discrimination & Research Obstruction Law

- **File:** `CANON/corpus/AI_Discrimination_Research_Obstruction_Law.pdf` (372 KB)
- **Extract:** `legal/_extracted/AI_Discrimination_Research_Obstruction_Law.txt` (705 lines)
- **Thesis:** Algorithmic throttling, undocumented usage caps, and automated "AI-only" support walls disproportionately obstruct neurodivergent and disabled independent safety researchers. The intersection of ADA Title III, §504 Rehabilitation Act, the Texas DTPA, and federal antitrust law creates a multi-defendant, class-action civil liability theory framing AI gatekeeping as a *de facto* cartelization of AI safety research.
- **Key doctrines:** ADA Title III ("auxiliary aids"); §504; Texas DTPA §17.46; Sherman Act §§1–2.
- **Relevance to JARVIS:** Evidence that the operator — himself neurodivergent and a disabled independent researcher — has a direct personal cause of action if vendor conduct obstructs his research; this is the civil-rights arm of the TinCan Firewall doctrine.

## 3. Anthropic Consumer Protection & Accessibility Lawsuit

- **File:** `CANON/corpus/Anthropic_Consumer_Protection_Accessibility_Lawsuit.pdf` (437 KB)
- **Extract:** `legal/_extracted/Anthropic_Consumer_Protection_Accessibility_Lawsuit.txt` (850 lines)
- **Thesis:** Anthropic PBC's Max 5x ($100/mo) and Max 20x ($200/mo) tiers, launched early 2026 and systemically degraded by undisclosed server-side changes ("Peak-Hour Multipliers," 20-tool call limit, rolling seven-day window) beginning late March 2026, give rise to (a) UCL §17200 bait-and-switch liability in California, (b) ADA Title III / §504 claims when the service is an auxiliary cognitive aid, (c) express-contract breach and breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.
- **Key doctrines:** California UCL §17200 & §17500; CLRA; ADA Title III; implied covenant.
- **Relevance to JARVIS:** Direct operator-facing grievance. This is the "what happened to us" case — the events that motivated the hardware-sovereignty hard invariant (`PRINCIPLES.md §2`).

## 4. Digital Subscription Legal Research

- **File:** `CANON/corpus/Digital_Subscription_Legal_Research.pdf` (375 KB)
- **Extract:** `legal/_extracted/Digital_Subscription_Legal_Research.txt` (717 lines)
- **Thesis:** Modern platform operators (Apple Developer Program; Anthropic LLM subscriptions) have been shielded by EULAs, arbitration clauses, and broad liability limits, but those shields are increasingly pierced by UCL, CLRA, ADA, ROSCA, and evolving post-sale duty-to-warn doctrine. Systemic verification latencies (Apple) and dynamic resource throttling (Anthropic) are two linked case studies; class certification is feasible when the platform conduct is uniform.
- **Key doctrines:** UCL §17200; CLRA; ROSCA; ADA Title III; post-sale duty to warn in SaaS.
- **Relevance to JARVIS:** Dual-track template — shows how the same conduct pattern (opaque throttling, unappealable automation) can be framed as consumer-protection and as accessibility violation on the same facts.

## 6. Texas DTPA vs. California Tech Law

- **File:** `CANON/corpus/Texas_DTPA_vs_California_Tech_Law.pdf` (470 KB) — *newly added 2026-04-20 from operator iCloud*
- **Extract:** `legal/_extracted/Texas_DTPA_vs_California_Tech_Law.txt` (847 lines)
- **Thesis:** Comparative analysis of the Texas DTPA (§17.41 *et seq.*) and the California UCL (§17200) for a Texas-resident plaintiff suing California-HQ'd tech defendants (Apple, Anthropic). Covers: "consumer" qualification under DTPA; actionable misrepresentations under §17.46(b)(5) and (b)(7); long-arm jurisdiction and forum-selection mechanics; damage modeling when the service functions as a compensatory cognitive aid (disability compounding).
- **Key doctrines:** Texas DTPA §17.41–§17.63 (especially §17.46(b)(5)/(7), §17.50 treble damages, §17.45(5) "unconscionable action"); California UCL §17200; long-arm personal jurisdiction (*International Shoe* / *Asahi* / *Bristol-Myers Squibb*); forum non conveniens.
- **Relevance to JARVIS:** The operator is Texas-resident and has the strongest choice-of-law position under Texas DTPA for mental-anguish and treble-damages recovery. This brief is the mechanics manual for that forum choice.
