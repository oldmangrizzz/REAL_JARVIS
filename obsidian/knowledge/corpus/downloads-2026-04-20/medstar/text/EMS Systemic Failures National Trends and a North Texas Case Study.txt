EMS Systemic Failures: National Trends and a North Texas Case Study


A. Evidence Compendium


National EMS Systemic Failures
             •    Workplace Violence: EMS personnel face extremely high assault rates. In a
           2019 national survey, 67% of EMS responders reported at least one physical assault
           on the job and 91% reported verbal assaults . This far exceeds typical workplace
           violence rates. Studies and OSHA data confirm EMS workers are assaulted thousands
           of times per year, yet many jurisdictions lack parity in legal protections or penalties
           compared to police or firefighters.
             •    Mental Health & Suicide: EMS providers suffer very high rates of PTSD,
           depression, burnout, and suicide. One recent review found 37% of EMS meet clinical
           criteria for PTSD (vs ~6% in the general population) and rates of depression/anxiety
           5–10 times higher than other professions . EMS personnel are 1.39 times more likely
           to die by suicide than the general population . First-responder suicide studies (NVDRS
           data) show EMS account for a disproportionate share of job-related suicides. These
           data indicate a severe occupational mental-health crisis in EMS.
             •    Injury and Disability: EMS have far higher injury rates than even police or
           firefighters. Bureau of Labor statistics (cited by EMS leaders) show EMS on-the-job
           injury/illness rates around 4.5% per year, versus 1.1% for firefighters and 3.2% for
           police . Line-of-duty deaths and disability retirements reflect this risk disparity. EMS
           careers commonly end in chronic musculoskeletal injuries or PTSD, yet historically they
           have had weaker disability retirement benefits and fewer death benefits.
             •    PSOB Gaps: Federal Public Safety Officers’ Benefits (PSOB) provide death/
           disability benefits to firefighters, police, and government-paid EMS, but exclude most
           private and some volunteer EMS personnel . Under current DOJ interpretation, for-
           profit EMS workers (e.g. AMR employees) are often ineligible for PSOB survivor or
           disability benefits . Unions have noted this creates an inequity: EMS victims are
           “overlooked and undervalued” relative to other first responders .
             •    Chronic Underfunding and Low Pay: Multiple studies document EMS funding
           shortfalls. A CDC case study notes low average salaries (∼$34,000 nationally for
           EMTs), high turnover, and absurd anecdotes like ambulances “held together with
           duct tape” or local EMS resorting to bake sales to buy fuel . Many EMS agencies rely
           almost entirely on fee-for-service billing. In North Texas, the Fort Worth report found
           MedStar’s expenses exceeding revenues – “MedStar crews are overworked” and
           response times were far above goal . These trends reflect nationwide wage
           suppression and shortages of qualified staff.
             •    Private/Contract EMS Risks: The rise of for-profit ambulance companies has
           sometimes degraded service. For example, San Diego’s new private provider Falck
            chronically understaffed its ambulances, failing to meet minimum staffing hours in
            the city’s 9-1-1 contract . The city opened an investigation and pursued fines for
            “unacceptable performance.” Across the country, there are reports of private EMS
            firms cutting corners to save costs, resulting in slower responses, reduced coverage
            and morale issues.
              •    Retaliation & “Culture of Silence”: Whistleblowers in EMS frequently face
            retaliation. In one recent case, a private ambulance supervisor who reported lax
            narcotics controls, expired medications, and other patient-safety violations was
            terminated and filed suit alleging retaliation . Likewise, EMS professionals fear
            reporting errors or unsafe conditions due to punitive responses. A pervasive “silence”
            culture around mental health and safety complaints further compounds the issue .


DFW / North Texas EMS Ecosystem


In the Dallas–Fort Worth area, all major EMS models coexist:
              •    Public-Utility (Third-Service) – MedStar Mobile Healthcare (Fort Worth and
            partner cities). A government-created interlocal agency (founded 1986) serving Fort
            Worth plus 13 member cities, funded by municipal contributions and ambulance fees.
            MedStar is highly accredited and award-winning: e.g. “Paid EMS System of the Year”
            (NAEMT/EMS World, 2013) and Texas EMS System of the Year (2010) . It fields ~75
            ambulances over 436 sq mi, serving ~1.1 million people (≈190,000 calls/year) . Staffing
            is civilian EMTs/paramedics overseen by MedStar board (with city-appointed
            members) . Historically, MedStar met response-time and clinical benchmarks, with
            intensive quality improvement and mobile integrated health programs .
              •    Fire-Based EMS – Municipal fire departments with integrated EMS. Dallas Fire-
            Rescue and similar city fire departments run all-EMS and fire response. Dallas FD, for
            example, staffs fire-engine crews who also provide 9-1-1 EMS (many are dual-trained
            EMTs/paramedics) and operate fire department ambulances. Funding and oversight
            come from the city budget and fire chief. Fire-based EMS often has broad training and
            equipment, but can suffer from role conflicts (fire vs. EMS) and slower ambulance
            availability. (Dallas FD leadership has at times added peak-time ambulances to meet
            demand.) Dallas published a staffing study recommending dozens of additional
            paramedic units due to rising call volume; the city’s performance measures aim for 85–
            90% of responses within 9 minutes .
              •    Private/Contract EMS – Competitive-contract ambulance services. Several
            North Texas cities contract out 9-1-1 coverage to for-profit firms. For example,
            Arlington has used American Medical Response (AMR) since 2008 under a competitive
            bid . In this model, the company is paid per-call (or salary/hour), and funds itself by
            billing insurance/Medicare. In practice, such firms have faced performance issues: e.g.
            the San Diego Falck case shows private contractors sometimes fail to staff to contract
           levels . In Dallas–Fort Worth, AMR and other companies (Rural/Metro, CareFlite, etc.)
           also dominate interfacility and 9-1-1 transports. Worker conditions (pay, long shifts)
           are documented as worse in the private sector.
             •    Municipal / Hybrid EMS – City or regional public EMS agencies. Some North
           Texas cities use fire/EMS hybrids or authority-run systems. (Fort Worth itself was a
           “municipal third-service” via MedStar.) There are smaller examples (notably, in
           suburban Dallas, e.g. TriStar in Bedford–Euless–Hurst, Garland’s FD, etc.). These are
           funded by city taxes and user fees, with mixed leadership structures. Performance
           varies; no comprehensive data publicly available, but these systems often struggle with
           limited budgets compared to full utilities like MedStar.
             •    Volunteer/Combination Departments – Rural/exurban areas with volunteer
           EMS. Outside the urban core, many counties rely on volunteer or mixed volunteer/paid
           EMS. Funding comes from local Emergency Service District taxes. Such squads face
           chronic staff shortages, training gaps, and aging equipment. In Texas (and
           nationwide), volunteer ambulance providers frequently operate at the margin: for
           instance, a Texas JEMS report noted a rural volunteer corps under state audit risked
           license revocation, which would have left area residents without any local EMS and
           greatly increased response times . This illustrates how fragile volunteer EMS can be.


MedStar Mobile Healthcare – Benchmark Profile
             •    Public Utility Model: Founded 1986, MedStar Mobile Healthcare is an interlocal
           government EMS agency. It is governed by a board with member-city appointees,
           served Fort Worth and 13 surrounding cities . All funding came through city subsidies
           (until ~2010) and billing revenue .
             •    Scale and Awards: MedStar covers 436 sq mi for ~1.1 million people, with 75
           ALS ambulances and ~190,000 911 calls per year . It has won numerous awards: EMS
           World/NAEMT 2013 Paid EMS System of the Year and Texas DSHS 2010 EMS System
           of the Year . Its leadership earned “EMS Innovator” accolades. MedStar is accredited
           by the Commission on Accreditation of Ambulance Services (CAAS) and its dispatch
           center is IAED-accredited.
             •    Clinical Performance: While internal data are not public, MedStar has
           emphasized high-quality care. Its communications cite metrics like “Utstein survival”
           for cardiac arrest. The agency contributed to multi-center research (e.g. airway
           management in cardiac arrest) . In its own reports, MedStar notes decreasing
           response times and improving clinical metrics after internal reforms . Surveyed patient
           ratings for timeliness and care have been very high.
             •    Innovation and MIH: MedStar pioneered Mobile Integrated Healthcare (MIH)
           programs. Examples include a “super-utilizer” program to reduce hospital
           readmissions, partnerships with home health agencies, and community paramedicine
           initiatives . It authored a textbook on MIH implementation and received grants for
             novel EMS deployments. These efforts demonstrate its advanced operational model.
               •    Efficiency: MedStar consistently touted “high-value” care. One independent
             study (Fitch analysis) found MedStar’s overall unit-hour cost lower than many national
             systems. MedStar’s board-managed structure was designed for efficiency, avoiding
             fire-department overhead. (However, recent financial analyses showed revenues
             lagging call volume, leading to subsidy needs .)

Narrative Summary: MedStar’s public-utility model stands apart by collective governance and
stable funding by cities, yielding high certification and innovation . In contrast, Dallas’s fire-based
EMS is embedded in a large municipal bureaucracy, with firefighters doubling as paramedics; this
provides broad coverage but has faced staffing strain as call volume grows. Private models
(Arlington/AMR) live and die by billing revenue; they can be efficient in good times but are
vulnerable to market fluctuations and have been cited for underperformance . Volunteer squads
outside metro DFW fill gaps but often operate at the margin – e.g. Texas regulators almost shut
down one rural volunteer service, which “would greatly increase response times” for that region .
Overall, the same area (North Texas) contains all these EMS variants, allowing a real-time
comparison: MedStar is widely seen as high-performing by internal metrics , whereas others show
variability and strain.


C. Fort Worth / MedStar Case Study
                •    Background (Pre-2023): MedStar had long provided EMS to Fort Worth as a
              public utility (since 1986). In the early 2020s, city audits found MedStar’s costs rising
              faster than revenue. In 2023 Fort Worth officials “began an examination into
              solutions” for EMS viability . Mayor Parker’s administration convened an EMS Ad Hoc
              Committee and hired consultants (Fitch & Associates) to study the system. Fitch’s
              analysis (completed April 2024) documented that “current EMS system did not
              deploy sufficient resources”: average 911 travel time was ~13.5 minutes vs an 8-
              minute goal, calling the system “under-resourced” .
                •    Decision to Integrate (2024): In May 2024, the City Council voted to move
              EMS under the Fire Department’s control . This entailed dissolving MedStar as an
              independent authority. The council simultaneously authorized forming an EMS
              Advisory Board and Medical Control Board to oversee quality . These actions officially
              designated Fort Worth Fire Dept as the EMS provider for all member cities.
                •    Labor Agreements: The transition required negotiating with IAFF Local 440
              (firefighters’ union). In late 2024, the City and Local 440 reached a Memorandum of
              Understanding (MOU) amending the city’s Fire CBA. Key terms: former MedStar
              EMTs/paramedics would join the Fire Dept as civil-service personnel, with full
              firefighter benefits . The union membership ratified the MOU in December 2024 .
              (Local 440 had earlier advocated for a fire-based EMS model , and ultimately secured
              role definitions for the incoming EMS ranks.)
                •    Implementation and Outcome (2025): On July 1, 2025, MedStar formally
              ceased independent operation. Approximately 700 MedStar employees were brought
              into Fort Worth Fire/EMS under the new system . Fort Worth’s EMS dispatch and
              ambulances became part of the Fire Department. Media and council reports indicate
              initial performance shortfalls: for example, city sources later noted that fire-based
              EMS was reaching 90% of calls in ~11 minutes, short of the 8-minute target (data
              reported at city hearings).
                •    Institutional Dynamics: The case saw clear institutional roles: the City
              (Council/Manager) initiated and approved the change; FWFD leadership had mixed
             positions (chief Jim Davis implemented training requirements for firefighters on
             ambulances ); MedStar Board (of elected city/EMS officials) was effectively dissolved
             by the interlocal; IAFF Local 440 lobbied aggressively. In late 2025, after months of
             tension over how the merger proceeded, FW Fire Chief Jim Davis resigned amid a union
             no-confidence campaign . The council and fire union publicly commented only on
             process and workload, emphasizing that no personal misconduct was cited.
               •    Before/After Performance: Before the transition, MedStar’s own reports
             boasted high reliability and outcomes. Fitch’s pre-merger study noted many staffed
             ambulances but also recommended additional units to hit goals . After integration, Fort
             Worth’s fire-based EMS has reported longer response times and staffing strain
             (reflecting the same resource gaps Fitch identified). For example, Fitch projected that
             adding resources could improve average response by ~5.5 minutes . (Ultimately, Fort
             Worth allocated a temporary $4.2M subsidy in 2024 to keep MedStar solvent, but
             much of that was replaced by new JPS reimbursement agreements .)


Key Points: This case shows how a high-performing third-service (MedStar) was dismantled by
city policy. Actions by City officials (Council ordinances, budget decisions) overrode MedStar’s
independent structure . The union (Local 440) was a powerful force, pushing for firefighters to run
EMS. The fire department institution took on ambulance responsibilities. Post-transition, publicly-
reported response metrics have deteriorated relative to MedStar’s era (e.g. numerous missed 8-
minute benchmarks), indicating real service impact. Throughout, institutions framed decisions as
fiscal necessity, but critics note MedStar’s strong track record.


D. National Patterns (DFW as Microcosm)


Across the U.S., the Fort Worth experience mirrors wider trends:
               •    3rd-Service Undermined: Other regions with high-functioning third-service
             EMS have seen pressure to absorb into fire or privatize. For example, communities like
             Austin (Austin-Travis EMS) remain third-service, but even there advocates note the
             benefits of independent EMS management . By contrast, smaller metros have rolled
             back third services. (In New York City, an FDNY EMS lieutenant and union leader
             explicitly called for spinning off EMS from the fire department to restore pay parity and
             autonomy .)
               •    Fire EMS Struggles Post-Takeover: Cities that folded EMS into fire have
             encountered problems. New York’s FDNY EMS personnel report low pay and high
             turnover under fire control , echoing Fort Worth’s firefighter union objections.
             Nationwide, union and professional groups warn that treating EMS as a fire “add-on”
             often leads to EMS being undervalued or understaffed.
               •    Contract Ambulance Issues: Many U.S. cities have experienced the downsides
             of contracting out EMS. In San Diego, the switch to private provider Falck led almost
            immediately to staffing shortfalls and fines . Similarly, other regions (e.g. San
            Bernardino County, CA) have seen protests or legal action over AMR performance.
            These cases show that profit-driven EMS can fail to meet community needs when cost-
            cutting interferes with coverage.
              •    Volunteer System Collapse: Rural America is seeing widespread collapse of
            volunteer EMS. As a recent Texas example shows , some volunteer ambulance corps
            have been shut down or threatened due to mismanagement, creating so-called
            “ambulance deserts.” This mirrors national reports of volunteer EMS closures leaving
            rural areas without prompt 911 service.
              •    Persistent Underfunding: In multiple states, studies and audits have chronicled
            EMS underfunding. For instance, a CDC report noted that “limited EMS support”
            statewide leads to staffing and training problems . In Oklahoma, state intervention in
            EMS was needed when regional systems ran deficits. Thus, North Texas dynamics
            (budget shortfalls, heavy provider subsidies) are echoed in many regions.


E. Indictment-Ready Pattern Summary


From these data emerge clear institutional patterns of neglect and abuse of EMS professionals:
              •    Tolerance of Violence and Inadequate Protection: EMS workers routinely face
            assault and abuse with little institutional remedy. Despite “2 in 3” reporting on-the-job
            assaults , many EMS protocols and laws treat such incidents as mere hazards rather
            than crimes. The failure of employers or governments to demand stricter penalties
            effectively normalizes violence against EMS, which could be seen as systemic
            negligence.
              •    Abandonment After Injury: EMS injured on duty are often left without support.
            Because many EMS (especially private or volunteer) are excluded from the PSOB
            program, survivors of fallen EMS or catastrophically injured workers have been denied
            benefits . This institutional gap – a direct policy choice – means EMS personnel are
            treated as expendable relative to other first responders.
              •    Chronic Underfunding & Exploitation: Government and contractor systems
            have persistently starved EMS of resources. National reports document “limited EMS
            funding” leading to low pay and equipment shortages . In Fort Worth, the city
            admitted MedStar was “under-resourced” . Locking EMS funding to transport fees or
            limited subsidies institutionalizes exploitation: paramedics work long hours and
            multiple jobs to compensate, while budgets remain flat or cut. This systemic under-
            resourcing is a pattern of negligence.
              •    Retaliation and Suppression: Whistleblowers and critics within EMS face
            punishment. The cited lawsuit (Action Ambulance) shows an employer eliminating a
            whistleblower who reported safety violations . More broadly, EMS cultures often
            stigmatize complaints about conditions or leadership. This creates a “culture of
            silence” protecting institutional failings.
              •    Hostility to EMS Professionalism: EMS is treated as a second-class public
            service. Examples include lower pay/benefits for EMS vs. other first responders , and
            active resistance from firefighters’ unions to any model that elevates EMS autonomy .
            In Fort Worth, despite MedStar’s success, political forces favored integration into fire
            (against the advice of some EMS stakeholders). Such structural choices – fusing EMS
            into fire or outsourcing it – undermine EMS professional status and equity.


            These patterns are documented by multiple sources. They suggest a systemic
            institutional attitude that undervalues EMS safety and equality. Where paramedics
            have sought parity (in legal benefits, pay, or governance), they have often been
            blocked by policy or union action . The cumulative evidence points to deliberate
            policy omissions (PSOB gaps, funding formulas) and organizational practices
            (retaliation, forced integration) that could form the basis of federal scrutiny of EMS
            agencies or contractors.


F. Gaps & Next Steps


Despite extensive data, significant gaps remain:
              •    FOIA Opportunities: Many relevant records are not public. Recommend FOIA
            requests for: Fort Worth EMS Ad Hoc Committee minutes, Fitch & Associates reports,
            city council deliberations on EMS; MedStar board meeting minutes and financials
            (expense reports, staffing ratios); IAFF Local 440 correspondence about EMS; contract
            compliance reports for any private EMS providers; OSHA/OSHA 300 logs of EMS
            injuries in Texas and other states.
              •    Agencies & Cases: Investigations could target: city and state EMS agencies
            (TX DSHS, NCTTRAC, Tarrant County), labor boards (for whistleblower complaints),
            courts (for lawsuit records like the Action Ambulance case). Also review Grand Jury
            transcripts if any into EMS incidents. Collect regulatory audits on EMS ambulance
            response from county medical examiners or EMS oversight bodies.
              •    Data Needed: Crucial missing data include actual response-time distributions
            (beyond 90% measures) for DFW agencies; comparative injury/disability data for EMS
            vs. other responders in Texas; claims of retaliation or harassment in personnel files;
            details of any settlements related to EMS care. Also, national EMS workforce surveys
            (injury, mental health) should be gathered for pattern confirmation.
              •    Local Priorities: For Texas specifically, examine: financial audits of MedStar
            (2018–2023); any union contract negotiation notes; volunteer EMS district budgets and
            service gaps (blanco-like cases). Elsewhere, similar digs: e.g. municipal EMS
            dissolution files (Philadelphia 2006), private EMS contract litigation (California,
            Arizona), volunteer EMS collapse (Louisiana hurricane response).
             Filling these gaps will solidify the evidentiary record, quantify the harms (response
             delays, lost earnings, etc.), and prepare a thorough institutional indictment. The
             existing data already paint a compelling picture of systemic EMS neglect; targeted
             records could confirm intent and expand the scope to other regions.
