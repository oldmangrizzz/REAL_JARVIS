The Architectural Evolution of
Prehospital Medicine: MedStar Mobile
Healthcare and the Genesis of Mobile
Integrated Healthcare
1. Introduction
The transformation of Emergency Medical Services (EMS) from a logistical transport
mechanism into a clinically integrated component of the healthcare value chain represents a
seminal shift in modern medical history. This evolution, now codified as Mobile Integrated
Healthcare (MIH), fundamentally challenges the operational and economic dogmas that
governed prehospital care for the latter half of the 20th century. While the intellectual lineage of
community-based paramedicine is geographically distributed—with notable contemporaneous
developments in North Carolina and Colorado—the specific economic and operational
architecture of the urban MIH model is inextricably linked to MedStar Mobile Healthcare in Fort
Worth, Texas.
This report provides an exhaustive historical and structural analysis of MedStar’s role in
pioneering MIH. It validates the assertion that MedStar served as the primary incubator for the
model’s transition from a clinical experiment to a sustainable business strategy. By examining
the convergence of the Public Utility Model (PUM), the rise of value-based care, and the specific
innovations of MedStar’s leadership between 2009 and 2013, we establish a definitive timeline
of how "you call, we haul" was replaced by patient-centered navigation. The analysis draws
upon historical data, operational protocols, and peer-reviewed outcomes to reconstruct the
emergence of a system that has since been replicated globally.

2. The Structural Preconditions: The Crisis of the
Traditional Model
To understand the radical nature of MedStar’s innovation, one must first dissect the structural
dysfunction of the American EMS landscape prior to 2009. The industry was—and in many
jurisdictions remains—trapped in a fee-for-service reimbursement cycle that incentivized volume
over value.

2.1 The Economic Perversity of "You Call, We Haul"
Since the release of the landmark 1966 white paper Accidental Death and Disability: The
Neglected Disease of Modern Society, EMS systems were engineered for speed and
stabilization. This engineering was reinforced by federal payment policies, specifically Medicare,
which reimbursed agencies only when a patient was physically transported to a hospital
emergency department (ED). This created a binary economic reality: transport generated
revenue; non-transport (treatment on scene, referral, or refusal) generated a loss.
Consequently, EMS agencies were financially motivated to transport every 911 caller, regardless
of clinical acuity. This model functioned adequately for trauma and cardiac arrest but failed
catastrophically when applied to the rising tide of chronic disease and social dysfunction that
began to overwhelm urban centers in the early 2000s. By the late 2000s, opposing
trends—increasing ED utilization and decreasing hospital capacity—created a bottleneck that
threatened the viability of emergency systems.

2.2 The Public Utility Model (PUM) Advantage
MedStar’s ability to deviate from this entrenched model was largely due to its unique
organizational structure. Established in 1986 through an interlocal agreement between Fort
Worth and surrounding cities, MedStar was formed as the Metropolitan Area EMS Authority
(MAEMSA). This creation was a direct response to a crisis of reliability among private providers
in the mid-1980s.
Crucially, MedStar adopted the Public Utility Model (PUM), a theoretical framework developed
by economist Jack Stout in the early 1980s. Stout’s model treated EMS not as a public safety
arm (like police or fire) but as a utility, governed by strict performance contracts and economic
efficiency. The PUM philosophy emphasized "System Status Management"—the dynamic
deployment of resources based on predictive demand analysis.
This structural DNA is vital to the MIH story. Because MedStar was a PUM, it possessed two
attributes that fire-based or purely private systems often lacked:
   1.​ Data-Centric Culture: The PUM relies on granular data to minimize response times and
       maximize unit hour utilization (UHU). MedStar had the analytical infrastructure to see
       patterns in patient utilization that others missed.
   2.​ Agility: As an administrative governmental agency managed by a board of directors from
       member cities, MedStar had the autonomy to experiment with deployment models without
       the bureaucratic rigidity of a municipal fire department or the profit-margin shackles of a
       publicly traded corporation.

2.3 The Discovery of the "Frequent Flier"
The catalyst for MedStar’s pivot occurred in preparation for the summer of 2009. Facing the
prospect of high call volumes due to Texas heat, the medical director and operations staff
initiated an audit of their 911 usage data to identify strain points. The analysis revealed a
shocking Pareto distribution: a microscopic cohort of patients was consuming a massive
proportion of resources.
Specifically, the data identified 21 individual patients who had generated nearly 800 ambulance
transports in a single 12-month period. These transports resulted in approximately $1 million in
billed charges, the vast majority of which was uncollectible due to the patients’ lack of insurance
or inability to pay.
This finding was the "smoking gun." It demonstrated that the traditional EMS model was not only
clinically ineffective for these patients—who were clearly not getting better despite hundreds of
ED visits—but was also a financial hemorrhage for the system and the community. The
realization that 21 people could drive $1 million in cost created the political and economic capital
necessary to attempt a radical experiment.
3. The Genesis of Intervention (2009–2011)
The transition from identifying the problem to operationalizing a solution marked the true
beginning of the MIH era at MedStar. Unlike later programs that would launch with federal
grants or hospital contracts, MedStar’s initiative began as an internal operational fix using
"found" resources.

3.1 The "Light Duty" Experiment
In July 2009, MedStar launched what would later be recognized as its first Community
Paramedicine pilot. Lacking a budget for new staff, the agency utilized a sunk cost: paramedics
who were on "light duty" status due to workplace injuries. These providers, unable to lift
stretchers or work 12-hour ambulance shifts, were traditionally relegated to administrative tasks.
MedStar leadership, including the Medical Director, repurposed these paramedics to conduct
proactive home visits to the identified super-utilizers. The hypothesis was straightforward: if a
paramedic could visit a "frequent flier" at home, assess their needs, and address social or
medical gaps, they might prevent the next 911 call.
The protocol was unprecedented. Paramedics, trained to work in 15-minute intervals of
high-adrenaline crisis, were now asked to sit in living rooms for an hour, reviewing medications
and discussing diet. The "light duty" medics brought a unique skill set—clinical knowledge
combined with the street-level problem-solving inherent to EMS. They acted not just as
clinicians but as forensic social workers, uncovering the root causes of 911 use: loneliness,
confusion over prescriptions, lack of transportation to primary care, or food insecurity.

3.2 Outcomes and Validation
The results of the light duty pilot were immediate. Among the target cohort, 911 utilization
dropped by approximately 77%. This reduction did not just save ambulance fuel; it liberated
ambulance capacity for true emergencies and reduced the burden on local emergency
departments.
This success validated two critical concepts:
  1.​ Paramedic as Navigator: It proved that paramedics could function effectively in a
      preventative role, leveraging the trust they already held in the community.
  2.​ Economic ROI: It demonstrated that avoiding a transport was financially superior to
      performing an unreimbursed transport.

3.3 The Broader Landscape: Wake County and Eagle County
To rigorously validate the claim of MedStar’s primacy, one must contextualize it against
contemporaneous developments. The year 2009 was a watershed moment for paramedicine,
with three distinct models emerging simultaneously:
   ●​ Wake County EMS (North Carolina): In January 2009, six months prior to MedStar’s
       pilot, Wake County launched its "Advanced Practice Paramedic" (APP) program. The
       APP model was designed primarily to support high-acuity calls (cardiac arrests) and
       secondarily to divert mental health and chronic crisis patients from EDs. While pioneering,
       the APP model was a clinical escalation—using higher-credentialed medics to provide
       more advanced care. MedStar acknowledged the Wake County model as a reference
     point but sought a different operational focus.
  ●​ Eagle County (Colorado): In 2010, the Western Eagle County Ambulance District
     (WECAD) launched the first rural Community Paramedic program. Their model was
     driven by access—filling the void of primary care in remote mountain communities. They
     became the first agency to license as a home health agency, focusing on rural healthcare
     delivery.
  ●​ MedStar (Texas): MedStar’s unique contribution was the system integration model.
     Unlike Wake (clinical support) or Eagle (rural access), MedStar focused on urban demand
     management and economic sustainability through payer partnerships. This distinction is
     vital: MedStar’s model was designed to solve the financial crisis of urban healthcare
     utilization, making it the blueprint for the "Mobile Integrated Healthcare" concept that
     would appeal to insurers and hospitals nationwide.

4. The Architects of Transformation
The evolution of MedStar’s program from an ad-hoc pilot to a globally recognized system was
not an accident of history but the result of deliberate leadership. A core team of executives and
clinicians coalesced around the vision of transforming the agency.

4.1 Matt Zavadsky, MS-HSA: The Evangelist
Matt Zavadsky, serving as the Chief Transformation Officer (and initially Director of Public
Affairs), became the public face of the MedStar model. Zavadsky’s contribution was strategic
communication. He possessed the ability to translate clinical outcomes into the language of
health economics. He championed the idea that EMS should not be paid for "driving," but for
"outcomes".
Zavadsky served as the Chair of the NAEMT Mobile Integrated Healthcare Committee, a
position that allowed him to export MedStar’s lessons to the national stage. He was instrumental
in distinguishing "Mobile Integrated Healthcare" from "Community Paramedicine," arguing that
the former represented a broader system change involving data and payer integration.

4.2 Doug Hooten, MBA: The Executive Sponsor
As CEO, Doug Hooten provided the organizational cover necessary for innovation. In a
risk-averse industry, Hooten sanctioned the use of agency resources for
non-revenue-generating activities (initially) in the belief that the long-term ROI would validate
the strategy. Hooten’s background in business administration, rather than purely clinical EMS,
allowed him to view MedStar as a healthcare enterprise. Together with Zavadsky, he
co-authored Mobile Integrated Healthcare – Approach to Implementation, which became the
definitive textbook for agencies attempting to replicate the model.

4.3 The Clinical Vanguard: Dr. Jeff Beeson and Dr. Neal Richmond
The regulatory barrier to MIH was significant: paramedics are licensed to treat emergencies.
Performing scheduled home visits required a reimagining of medical direction. MedStar’s
Medical Directors, Dr. Jeff Beeson and later Dr. Neal Richmond, were pivotal in writing the
protocols that legalized these interactions.
Dr. Beeson, in particular, provided the foundation upon which the MIH protocols were built,
allowing paramedics to assess, treat, and refer patients without transport—a deviation from
standard "treat and transport" guidelines that required immense clinical trust. Dr. Richmond
continued this work, emphasizing the "Triple Aim" and ensuring the clinical rigor of the
interventions.

4.4 Operational Pioneers: Desiree Partain, Brandon Pate, and John
Farris
The theoretical model was operationalized by a cadre of field staff who invented the practice of
urban community paramedicine in real-time.
  ●​ Desiree Partain: Starting as a paramedic in 2009, Partain rose to become the Mobile
      Integrated Healthcare Manager. She was the operational architect, translating the
      high-level strategy into daily workflows. She managed the complex logistics of
      coordinating with hospital case managers, securing patient appointments, and overseeing
      the "light duty" staff. Her tenure represents the professionalization of the MIH manager
      role.
  ●​ Brandon Pate: As a Critical Care Paramedic and later Assistant Operations Manager,
      Pate pioneered the integration of geriatric care principles into the MIH model. He
      developed fall prevention screenings and collaborated with academic partners at UNT
      Health Science Center to bring rigorous quality improvement methodologies to the
      program.
  ●​ John Farris: One of the earliest community paramedics, Farris exemplified the shift in
      provider mentality. His work with high utilizers—often involving "tough love" and
      accountability—demonstrated that the "soft skills" of communication and empathy were as
      critical as clinical acumen in reducing 911 dependence.

5. Defining "Mobile Integrated Healthcare": The 2013
Pivot
A critical juncture in this history is the deliberate rebranding and semantic shift that occurred
around 2013. This was not merely a marketing exercise but a strategic reorientation of the
agency’s identity.

5.1 From "EMS" to "Mobile Healthcare"
In 2013, the Metropolitan Area EMS Authority officially changed its trade name from "MedStar
EMS" to "MedStar Mobile Healthcare". This change was driven by the realization that "EMS"
implied a limitation: Emergency. By adopting "Mobile Healthcare," MedStar signaled to payers,
hospitals, and the public that their scope of practice had expanded to include unscheduled and
scheduled primary care, chronic disease management, and navigation.
This rebranding coincided with a broader industry push, led by MedStar and NAEMT, to adopt
the term Mobile Integrated Healthcare (MIH) over "Community Paramedicine." The distinction
was nuanced but important: "Community Paramedicine" described a provider type; "Mobile
Integrated Healthcare" described a system of care that integrated EMS resources with the
broader healthcare grid.

5.2 The High Utilizer Group (HUG) and "EMS Loyalty"
The flagship of this new identity was the "EMS Loyalty Program," later formalized as the High
Utilizer Group (HUG). The term "Loyalty Program" was a tongue-in-cheek internal reference to
the PUM’s customer-service orientation—these patients were "loyal" to 911, and MedStar
sought to reward that loyalty with a better service than a lights-and-sirens ride.
The HUG program targeted patients calling 911 fifteen or more times in 90 days. The
intervention was high-touch and low-tech: regular home visits to teach patients how to manage
their conditions and navigate the healthcare system. The success of HUG—often reducing
utilization by over 80%—became the proof-of-concept that attracted external funding.

5.3 The CHF Readmission Avoidance Program
In 2012, the Affordable Care Act (ACA) introduced the Hospital Readmissions Reduction
Program (HRRP), penalizing hospitals for excessive readmissions of patients with Congestive
Heart Failure (CHF), pneumonia, and AMI. This policy change created a market opportunity for
MedStar.
MedStar approached local hospitals (such as JPS Health Network) with a proposition: pay
MedStar to visit discharged CHF patients at home to prevent readmission. This was a radical
departure from the status quo. Instead of being paid to transport a patient to the hospital,
MedStar was now being paid to keep them out. This program was the first major instance of an
EMS agency securing a contract to reduce hospital revenue (by preventing admissions) in
exchange for shared savings, effectively aligning EMS incentives with healthcare reform goals.

5.4 Nurse Triage and Upstream Intervention
Recognizing that many 911 calls were low-acuity (e.g., toothaches, minor abrasions), MedStar
integrated Registered Nurses (RNs) into the 911 communications center. Using the Emergency
Communication Nurse System (ECNS), MedStar began diverting eligible calls to nurse triage.
These nurses could offer self-care advice or arrange alternative transport (like Lyft/Uber
partnerships) to clinics, preserving ambulance resources for life-threatening emergencies. This
"911 Nurse Triage" became another pillar of the MIH ecosystem, further validating the "Mobile
Healthcare" moniker.

6. The Economic Architecture: The "Triple Aim"
The sustainability of MedStar’s model rested on its ability to articulate value. The traditional
EMS model is a negative-margin business for non-transport activities; a paramedic visit without
a ride to the hospital generates zero revenue under standard Medicare Part B rules.

6.1 Operationalizing the Triple Aim
MedStar leadership adopted the Institute for Healthcare Improvement’s (IHI) Triple
Aim—improving the patient experience, improving population health, and reducing per capita
costs—as their strategic North Star. By framing their services within this construct, they were
able to speak the language of hospital CFOs and insurance executives.
MedStar demonstrated that a $300 community paramedic visit was a rational investment if it
prevented a $10,000 hospital admission. This economic logic allowed them to secure contracts
with commercial payers and hospital systems, creating a diversified revenue stream that
insulated the MIH program from the vagaries of grant funding.

6.2 The StarSaver Membership
An often-overlooked component of MedStar’s financial integration was the StarSaver
membership program. While primarily an ambulance subscription service to cover out-of-pocket
costs for transport, the existence of such a membership base provided a stable relationship with
the community. In 2024, the City of Fort Worth transitioned this program, but for decades, it
represented a direct financial link between MedStar and the residents it served, reinforcing the
"utility" aspect of the model.

7. Barriers and Breakthroughs
The path to establishing MIH was fraught with obstacles that MedStar had to systematically
dismantle.

7.1 The Reimbursement Cliff
The most significant barrier was the lack of reimbursement codes for MIH services. In the early
years (2009-2012), MedStar relied entirely on internal savings. By reducing the call volume from
super-utilizers, they reduced the operational tempo, saving fuel and wear-and-tear on the fleet.
This "cost avoidance" strategy was the bridge that allowed them to survive until external payer
contracts could be negotiated. This bootstrapping approach proved that MIH could be viable
even without immediate fee-for-service revenue, a critical lesson for other agencies.

7.2 "Scope of Practice" vs. "Scope of Employment"
Critics often argued that community paramedicine exceeded the EMS scope of practice.
MedStar navigated this by clarifying that the clinical skills (assessment, vital signs, history
taking) were well within a paramedic's scope; it was the context (home visit vs. roadside) and
the goal (management vs. transport) that had changed. By working strictly under the delegated
authority of the Medical Director, MedStar avoided regulatory pitfalls that doomed other
programs.

7.3 Cultural Resistance
Internally, shifting the culture of a high-volume urban EMS system was challenging. Paramedics
are culturally conditioned to value high-acuity interventions—intubations, cardiac arrests,
trauma. The "slow medicine" of MIH—spending an hour discussing diet—was initially viewed
with skepticism. MedStar addressed this by selecting "light duty" staff who had no choice but to
participate, and then letting their success stories (like the transformation of Antoine Hall) convert
the skeptics. Over time, the "Hope Squad" and other internal initiatives helped integrate the
mental health and wellness of the providers into the broader culture of care.

8. Validation and Legacy
The validation of MedStar’s model came through data. A 2017 peer-reviewed study in the
American Journal of Emergency Medicine, co-authored by MedStar staff and academic
researchers, provided the empirical proof the industry needed. The study showed that patients
in the MIH program experienced significant improvements in quality of life (mobility, self-care)
and drastic reductions in ED utilization.
The Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) profiled MedStar’s program multiple
times, citing it as a model for the nation. The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS)
eventually used lessons from MedStar (and others) to design the ET3 (Emergency Triage,
Treat, and Transport) model, a federal pilot aimed at reimbursing EMS for non-transport
interventions—a direct policy descendant of the work begun in Fort Worth.

9. Conclusion
The investigation decisively confirms that while the concept of paramedicine beyond the
ambulance was emerging globally, MedStar Mobile Healthcare in Fort Worth, Texas, is the
originator of the Mobile Integrated Healthcare business model.
MedStar did not merely implement a clinical program; they engineered a new sector of the
healthcare economy. By leveraging the data-rich environment of the Public Utility Model, the
leadership of visionaries like Matt Zavadsky and Doug Hooten, and the operational agility of
their staff, MedStar transformed a local "frequent flier" problem into a national solution for
healthcare value. They coined the terminology, wrote the textbooks, established the payer
contracts, and provided the data that allowed MIH to transition from a fringe experiment to a
standard of care. The history of MedStar’s program is the history of EMS claiming its seat at the
table of modern medicine.
