Forensic Analysis of the MedStar to Fort
Worth Fire Department Emergency
Medical Services Transition
Executive Summary
The dissolution of the Area Metropolitan Ambulance Authority, operating as MedStar Mobile
Healthcare, and its subsequent absorption into the Fort Worth Fire Department, represents a
fundamental paradigm shift in municipal emergency medical services. Executed in July 2025,
this operational transfer dismantled a decades-old Public Utility Model in favor of a traditional,
monolithic fire-based delivery system. This forensic intelligence report provides an exhaustive,
longitudinal analysis of this transition, tracing the deep-rooted systemic variables that
precipitated the shift and the subsequent operational fallout.
By examining the historical operational baseline spanning from 1986 to 2009, the administrative
mechanics that forced the transition between 2020 and 2025, and the immediate post-transition
performance audit through March 2026, a clear and highly concerning trajectory emerges. The
empirical data indicates that the shift has resulted in severe systemic friction across virtually
every operational and clinical metric. This friction is evidenced by a highly accelerated
personnel attrition rate, critical physical infrastructure shortages, significantly degraded
response time compliance, and a massive transfer of financial burden onto the municipal
taxpayer.
The ensuing comprehensive study isolates the specific variables, political mechanics, and
logistical realities driving the current operational degradation. It evaluates the transition not
merely as a change in municipal letterhead, but as a profound structural alteration to the way
pre-hospital clinical medicine is delivered, managed, and financed across a
four-hundred-and-thirty-six square mile jurisdiction. The findings synthesize historical precedent
with current administrative realities to provide a definitive audit of an emergency medical system
in acute distress.

Part I: Historical Operational Baseline (1986–2009)
The Public Utility Model Architecture and Pre-2006 Efficiency
To accurately measure the magnitude and the consequences of the 2025 transition, it is
absolutely imperative to establish a rigorous historical baseline of the system that preceded it. In
1986, the City of Fort Worth, functioning in collaboration with surrounding municipalities,
established the Area Metropolitan Ambulance Authority to oversee MedStar. This system was
meticulously engineered utilizing the Public Utility Model, a highly specialized framework
conceptualized in the early 1980s by emergency medical services economist Jack Stout.
Under the Public Utility Model, the local government acts as an exclusive purchaser of services,
while a private or quasi-governmental entity manages the daily operations, logistics, and
personnel. The fundamental philosophical premise of the Public Utility Model is the strict
separation of clinical oversight, asset ownership, and labor management to maximize economic
efficiency and protect patient care. Within this architecture, MedStar operated as a failsafe
franchise. The system was deliberately designed to insulate clinical medical delivery from the
political, union, and financial pressures that are typically inherent in direct municipal department
management. The independent Office of the Medical Director maintained strict, uncompromising
clinical control, while the operational contractor was held to severe, contractually binding
performance metrics. Failure to meet specific response time fractiles resulted in substantial
financial penalties, ensuring that system efficiency was inexorably linked to operational survival.
When evaluating the efficiency of the quasi-governmental and contractor-led architecture used
prior to 2006, the financial data reveals a system operating at peak economic optimization.
During its early and middle operational eras, MedStar received minor, highly regulated subsidies
from Fort Worth and its participating member cities. However, these subsidies were a fraction of
the cost required to operate a fully municipalized service. Through aggressive optimization of
the medical billing cycle, maximization of commercial insurance and Medicare reimbursements,
and the implementation of advanced operational protocols, the system's reliance on municipal
taxpayer subsidies was steadily engineered out of existence.
The contrast between the taxpayer subsidy requirements of this early era and the 2026
fire-based budget is staggering. By 2010, MedStar had achieved complete financial
independence, operating without any direct public subsidy from the City of Fort Worth or its
member cities for over a decade. A third-party cost reporting firm validated that MedStar
operated as the lowest-cost provider in the State of Texas between 2012 and 2022. In 2024, the
expense per transport for MedStar was calculated at four hundred and ninety-six dollars. This
stood in profound contrast to the state average for fire-based ambulance systems, which
operated at a massively inflated cost of two thousand, three hundred and sixty-four dollars per
transport. The historical baseline dictates that prior to the post-pandemic inflationary crisis, the
Public Utility Model operated with maximal financial efficiency, effectively shielding the municipal
general fund from the volatile, high-risk economics of healthcare logistics. In direct contrast, the
2026 fire-based budget assumes an immediate operating cost of 89.2 million dollars, introducing
a severe permanent liability to the municipal ledger.

Self-Governance Evolution (2006–2009) and System Status
Management
As the Public Utility Model matured, MedStar underwent a significant evolution toward a
self-governed Area Metropolitan Ambulance Authority model between 2006 and 2009. This
transition marked a critical shift in how emergency medical services were deployed across the
region. Recognizing that efficiency relied on economies of scale, the system heavily invested in
the optimization of dynamic resource allocation. The clinical and operational efficiency of the
self-governed MedStar era was largely driven by the aggressive implementation of System
Status Management.
To understand the profound operational shift that occurred during this period, one must contrast
System Status Management with legacy deployment methodologies. Traditional fire department
operations rely entirely on a static deployment model. In a static model, resources are housed in
fixed geographic locations—specifically, expensive brick-and-mortar fire stations—where crews
wait passively for emergency calls to be dispatched. System Status Management, conversely, is
a purely dynamic deployment strategy. It utilizes highly granular, historical computer-aided
dispatch data to predict the temporal and geographic probability of emergency calls before they
even happen.
Under the rules of System Status Management, ambulances do not sit idle in fixed stations.
Instead, they are continuously routed and posted at strategic street-corner locations,
commercial parking lots, or high-traffic arterial corridors based on real-time system demand. As
call volume fluctuates throughout the day, and as units are committed to hospital transports, the
remaining available ambulances are dynamically and continuously repositioned to maintain an
optimal geographic net of coverage over the city.
This methodology fundamentally altered MedStar's infrastructure requirements and represents
the peak clinical era of the system's history. Because ambulances were constantly in motion or
posted in dynamic, data-driven locations, the necessity for fixed ambulance stations was
effectively eliminated. This shift in operational philosophy culminated in the permanent closure
of fixed physical assets, most notably the shuttering of Station 33 around 2009.
The closure of Station 33 was not a failure of the system, nor was it a symptom of financial
distress; rather, it was a calculated, highly intelligent logistical optimization. Capital that would
have been wasted on real estate maintenance, facility overhead, and static operational drag
was instead redirected directly into frontline clinical assets, advanced fleet maintenance, and
the development of mobile integrated healthcare programs. The closure of Station 33
symbolized the complete maturation of MedStar into a fully dynamic, high-performance
emergency medical service. The system proved that superior clinical response times could be
achieved not by building more stations, but by utilizing predictive algorithms to place
ambulances exactly where they were statistically most likely to be needed. The abandonment of
this philosophy in 2025 serves as the root cause for the operational paralysis currently plaguing
the Fort Worth Fire Department.

Part II: The "Manufactured Crisis" & Transition
(2020–July 2025)
The Fitch & Associates Critique and the Financial Disparity
The financial stability of the MedStar Public Utility Model began to erode significantly in the
wake of the global macroeconomic disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic.
Skyrocketing personnel costs, severe supply chain inflation, and entirely stagnant
reimbursement rates from commercial insurance, Medicare, and Medicaid created an
unavoidable structural deficit. While operational costs surged, the revenue generated per
transport remained artificially suppressed by federal healthcare policy. By 2023, MedStar
required transitional funding to maintain its high-performance operations, approaching the city
with a request for approximately four million to six million dollars in municipal subsidies.
In response to this request, the Fort Worth City Council characterized the situation as an
existential crisis and commissioned Fitch & Associates, an independent consulting firm, to
conduct an exhaustive assessment of the emergency medical services system to propose
alternative future models. A deep dive into the April 2024 Fitch report reveals the specific data
that challenges the narrative of a necessary transition.
The Fitch analysis provided four distinct operational pathways. The financial disparity between
these pathways is the most critical data point in understanding the political nature of the ensuing
transition. The consultant's objective analysis concluded that retaining the MedStar
system—updating the governance structure to grant more direct control to the Fort Worth City
Council while maintaining the independent operational architecture—would require an estimated
taxpayer subsidy of merely 3.2 million dollars for the first year of public funding. Fitch explicitly
noted that this retention model would be the easiest to implement, as it maintained the greatest
degree of the status quo and preserved the highly optimized System Status Management
deployment.
Conversely, the Fitch report provided explicit cautions regarding the exorbitant costs associated
with abandoning the Public Utility Model in favor of a municipal fire-based model. The report
projected that transitioning the ambulance service entirely into the Fort Worth Fire Department
would cost between 10.5 million and 63 million dollars in direct implementation costs and initial
subsidies, depending heavily on how MedStar's physical assets were absorbed and whether the
city utilized civilian single-role personnel or sworn dual-role firefighters.
Despite this massive, documented financial disparity—and despite Fitch warning that a
transition of this magnitude would carry significant, unpredictable clinical and operational
implications—the Fort Worth City Council voted unanimously in May 2024 to dissolve MedStar
and absorb its operations into the fire department. The administrative decision bypassed a
highly economical, clinically proven 3.2 million dollar retention option in favor of a system that
would ultimately require an annual municipal operating budget of 89.2 million dollars. While
projected billing revenues were estimated to offset roughly 65 million dollars of this cost, the
new model immediately shackled the city with a structural deficit vastly larger than the minor 4
million dollar subsidy MedStar initially requested to survive the post-pandemic inflation. This
sequence of events strongly supports the analytical conclusion that the financial distress of
MedStar was leveraged as a "manufactured crisis," utilized to justify a predetermined political
objective to municipalize the service regardless of the fiscal logic.

Union & Administrative Mechanics: The Role of Local 440
The dissolution of the Area Metropolitan Ambulance Authority cannot be analyzed purely
through an economic or clinical lens; it was heavily driven by internal administrative mechanics
and sophisticated labor union strategy. The International Association of Fire Fighters (IAFF)
Local 440, which represents the Fort Worth Professional Firefighters Association, aggressively
supported and lobbied for the transition to a fire-based emergency medical services model.
Local 440 played a paramount role in shaping the transition's framework. The union actively
negotiated the Memorandums of Understanding and the collective bargaining agreements that
would define how the absorbed MedStar paramedics, emergency medical technicians, and
telecommunicators would be classified within the city's civil service structure. By bringing over
six hundred private-sector healthcare workers into the municipal union fold, Local 440
significantly expanded its membership base, dues revenue, and political leverage within the city.
The transition framework required the creation of single-role civil service positions within the fire
department, ensuring these new employees received municipal pensions and benefits aligned
with the fire collective bargaining agreement.
However, the rapid integration of over six hundred former MedStar employees into a traditional,
insular firehouse culture immediately generated profound institutional friction. This cultural and
operational fracture culminated in a dramatic administrative collapse: the abrupt resignation of
Fort Worth Fire Chief Jim Davis.

The Timeline of Chief Jim Davis's Resignation
Chief Jim Davis, who had successfully led the Fort Worth Fire Department since October 2018,
was initially tasked with overseeing the execution of the MedStar merger. The official transition
date when MedStar employees became city personnel was July 1, 2025. Almost immediately
following this integration, severe operational tensions arose regarding medic training, transition
speed, and safety protocols.
In late August 2025, just weeks after the merger, Chief Davis issued an operational mandate
requiring all legacy firefighters to undergo driver training to operate the newly acquired
ambulance fleet. This directive was rooted in the operational necessity of managing the massive
influx of medical volume; the new medical division was fielding between five hundred and six
hundred calls per day, and cross-training was essential to maintain system fluidity. However,
Local 440 leadership pushed back aggressively. Members of the association had long
advocated for keeping their traditional firefighting duties strictly separate from the grueling,
high-volume reality of emergency medical transport. Outside experts warned that assigning
legacy firefighters to ambulance duty would greatly increase their workload, leading to severe
union pushback against Davis's administration.
Simultaneously, Davis documented grave concerns regarding departmental safety standards.
Following a severe incident where a firefighter was nearly killed by a collapsing roof, Chief Davis
pushed for the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) to launch an
external, independent investigation into the department's safety practices. The Fort Worth
Professional Firefighters Association firmly opposed this external review, creating an
insurmountable rift between the Fire Chief and the union leadership.
The timeline accelerated rapidly. On September 17, 2025, yielding to mounting pressure from
the union, the City of Fort Worth quietly placed Chief Davis on paid administrative leave. As
Davis languished on leave, Local 440 finalized preparations for a formal vote of no-confidence
against his leadership. Recognizing that his position had become untenable due to union
interference regarding his medic training mandates and safety audits, Chief Davis negotiated a
separation agreement. He formally tendered his resignation on October 17, 2025, with an
effective departure date of October 31, 2025. The city paid Davis a ninety-nine thousand dollar
settlement to drop his complaints. This administrative decapitation removed the primary
architect of the transition precisely as the operational complexities and clinical failures of the
merger began to manifest in the field.

Part III: Post-Transition Performance & "Systemic
Abuse" Audit (July 2025–March 2026)
The Raymond Hill Briefing: A System in Distress
Following the forced resignation of Chief Davis, Assistant Chief Raymond Hill was elevated to
the position of Interim Fire Chief. The operational reality of the absorbed emergency medical
services division was starkly detailed during a pivotal briefing delivered by Interim Chief Hill to
the Fort Worth City Council Public Safety Committee on March 3, 2026. This testimony serves
as a definitive, on-the-record audit of the post-transition system. The data presented by Hill
reveals an organization suffering from a rapid loss of personnel, systemic fatigue, and collapsing
clinical metrics.

Unit Hour Utilization (UHU) and the 24-Hour Shift
A core component of the operational crisis revolves around Unit Hour Utilization (UHU). UHU is
the standard, objective metric used globally to gauge the workload and fatigue levels of
emergency medical crews. It is calculated by dividing the time an ambulance spends actively
engaged on emergency tasks by the total hours the unit is staffed. During the transition
planning, the EMS Ad Hoc Committee established an assumption that the system would
operate at a 0.50 Unit Hour Utilization target.
Operating at a 0.50 UHU implies that a paramedic is actively engaged in high-stress,
critical-task emergency response for 50 percent of their scheduled shift. The Fitch & Associates
report explicitly cautioned against this, stating that an upper unit utilization threshold of
approximately 0.30 to 0.40 is considered best practice to prevent clinical errors and provider
burnout.
To mitigate severe staffing shortages, the department announced during the March 2026
briefing that it was implementing a pilot program to change the EMS deployment from dynamic
twelve-hour shifts to static twenty-four-hour shifts (specifically, a twenty-four hours on, forty-eight
hours off model) starting March 7, 2026. Applying a 0.50 UHU target to a twenty-four-hour shift
is clinically catastrophic. It dictates that medical providers are expected to perform twelve hours
of active, adrenaline-driven task time within a single continuous operational period. There is a
direct, thoroughly documented clinical correlation between a 0.50 UHU on a twenty-four-hour
shift and severe provider fatigue, degraded cognitive function, medication calculation errors, and
an exponentially higher risk of vehicular accidents. This shift structure represents systemic
abuse of the workforce, prioritizing geographical coverage optics over the physiological limits of
the human medical providers.

Dynamic Hybrid Deployment: The Worst of Both Worlds
The transition forced the Fort Worth Fire Department to attempt to merge two fundamentally
incompatible operational philosophies: the dynamic System Status Management of MedStar
and the static, brick-and-mortar reality of the fire department. The result is a highly inefficient
"Dynamic Hybrid Deployment" model.
In this reality, single-role medics are assigned to a specific fire station where they must
physically clock in. However, because call volume demands dynamic coverage, these crews are
frequently not allowed to remain at the station. Instead, they experience the "Station
Clock-in/Street Posting" reality, where they report to a static facility only to be immediately
deployed to sit in an ambulance at a street corner or parking lot to await calls, mimicking the old
System Status Management framework but introducing massive operational drag. This hybrid
model strips the medics of the amenities of the fire station while forcing them into a perpetual
state of transit, severely degrading morale and delaying mobilization times as crews must
constantly commute from peripheral stations inward to dynamic high-demand posts.

Staffing Attrition Analysis: The Death Spiral Markers
The systemic friction of the transition, combined with severe fatigue and cultural alienation, has
triggered a severe staffing crisis. Interim Chief Hill's March 2026 testimony confirmed that the
emergency medical services branch is currently trapped in an accelerated attrition cycle. The
division is losing an average of seven staff members every single month, resulting in a shocking
16 percent attrition rate. This figure represents a "death spiral" marker, especially when
contrasted against the historical 5 percent attrition rate typically observed within the broader
Fort Worth Fire Department.
The staffing mathematics presented during the briefing expose the depth of the crisis. The
emergency medical services division was designed with a target staffing matrix of four hundred
and forty-nine personnel. As of March 2026, the department possessed only three hundred and
sixty active personnel on the roster. Critically, of those three hundred and sixty individuals,
seventy-four were either locked in academy training or were recent graduates unqualified for
immediate street deployment. This leaves the system with a staggering eighty-nine critical
vacancies on the front lines. Interim Chief Hill explicitly noted that the majority of departing
single-role medical providers are abandoning the Fort Worth system to seek dual-role positions
in competing agencies, indicating a widespread rejection of the newly formed organizational
culture.
To keep ambulances on the street despite these eighty-nine vacancies, the department has
resorted to chronic, systemic mandated overtime. A department spokesperson admitted to the
Public Safety Committee that leadership is currently executing "force-hires" of approximately ten
individuals every single day. Under the previous schedule, medics were subjected to relentless
mandatory holdovers, resulting in a baseline requirement of at least four hours of mandatory
overtime per person, per cycle. This frequency of force-hire overtime is the primary catalyst
driving the 16 percent attrition rate, creating a self-perpetuating cycle where fatigue causes
resignations, which in turn causes more forced overtime for the remaining staff.

Performance Metrics: The Degradation of Response Times
The inevitable, clinical consequence of high attrition, forced overtime, and inefficient hybrid
deployment is the severe degradation of response times to the public. During the Public Utility
Model era, MedStar was contractually bound to aggressive, fractile response metrics,
historically achieving a 90 percent compliance rate for high-priority, life-threatening emergency
calls within an eight-minute benchmark. The primary political justification presented by city
leadership for dismantling MedStar was the promise that a fire-based department could improve
upon these metrics.
Empirical data from early 2026 confirms that the fire-based system is comprehensively failing to
meet this objective. Records indicate that the new emergency medical services division is hitting
the 90 percent threshold at eleven minutes and nine seconds, missing the vital eight-minute
goal by over three full minutes. The mathematical distribution of these figures suggests that at
the strict eight-minute mark, the operational compliance rate has plummeted to roughly 64
percent. By the city's own internal estimates, the combined response times of the fire-based
system are performing thirty seconds slower on average than the times recorded by MedStar in
the year preceding the municipal takeover. This performance degradation nullifies the primary
administrative argument that transitioning away from System Status Management would yield
faster clinical interventions.

Part IV: Infrastructure & Culture Integrity
Logistical Readiness: The "Bed Shortage" Admission
The most glaring manifestation of poor transition planning is found in the physical infrastructure
of the fire department. The sudden shift to the twenty-four-hour shift model in March 2026
required hundreds of former MedStar employees to live, eat, and sleep inside municipal fire
stations. Because MedStar utilized System Status Management and posted ambulances
dynamically on street corners, it did not possess residential stations with sleeping quarters.
Therefore, the fire department was forced to absorb these massive crews into legacy firehouses
that were never architecturally designed for such a population density.
During his March 2026 testimony, Interim Chief Hill made a stunning admission regarding
logistical readiness: the department did not possess enough beds for its absorbed medical
workforce. Despite having two months to prepare for the 24/48 shift pilot program, the
administration failed to procure adequate sleeping quarters. Hill confirmed on the public record
that frontline paramedics and emergency medical technicians would be forced to sleep on
temporary cots for at least the first two months of the new shift cycle while permanent beds
remained back-ordered.
Furthermore, the department failed to secure and install adequate privacy partitions in the
stations. This logistical failure represents a severe cultural oversight, particularly given that the
single-role emergency medical services demographic contains a significantly higher percentage
of female providers than the legacy, male-dominated dual-role firefighting staff. Forcing
exhausted, force-hired medical professionals to sleep on temporary cots in open, unpartitioned
bays fundamentally destroys cultural integrity, strips the workforce of basic dignity, and directly
accelerates the 16 percent attrition rate.

The Service Area Disparity: The Impossibility of Static Deployment
Beyond the internal station logistics, the integration of MedStar into the Fort Worth Fire
Department created an unsolvable geometric paradox regarding the service area. Historically,
the Fort Worth Fire Department provided first-responder and suppression services strictly within
the city limits of Fort Worth, covering a geographic footprint of roughly three hundred and fifty to
three hundred and fifty-nine square miles. Their fire stations are distributed specifically to cover
this defined municipal geometry.
However, the interlocal agreements executed during the MedStar dissolution mandate that the
Fort Worth Fire Department must serve as the primary transport agency not just for Fort Worth,
but for fourteen additional member cities. This expands the total mandatory service area to four
hundred and thirty-six square miles.
Attempting to cover four hundred and thirty-six square miles using a static, station-based
deployment model designed for three hundred and fifty square miles is a literal physical
impossibility without drastically expanding the fleet and constructing dozens of new stations in
the member cities. MedStar covered this vast, four-hundred-and-thirty-six square mile
geographic footprint efficiently precisely because it did not rely on fixed stations; utilizing System
Status Management, ambulances were fluidly relocated across inter-jurisdictional borders as
demand shifted. By eliminating pure System Status Management and forcing ambulances to
base out of Fort Worth's legacy fire stations, the department has created massive geographical
blind spots in the outer member cities. This service area disparity guarantees that units must
travel farther distances from fixed points to reach the periphery, mathematically cementing the
eleven-minute and nine-second delayed response times.

Financial Per Annum Burden: Orders of Magnitude Calculation
The economic transition from the self-sustaining Area Metropolitan Ambulance Authority to the
Fort Worth Fire Department represents an exponential, entirely voluntary transfer of debt onto
the municipal taxpayer. To calculate the true magnitude of this financial burden, the historical
pre-2024 baseline must be directly contrasted with the FY2026 operational reality.
Between 2010 and 2022, the Public Utility Model utilized by MedStar required an effective per
annum taxpayer subsidy of zero dollars. The system was a self-contained economic engine,
funded entirely through fee-for-service revenue mechanisms. When post-pandemic inflation and
stagnant Medicaid rates finally destabilized this model in 2023, MedStar projected a
requirement of approximately 3.2 million to 4.2 million dollars in annual municipal subsidies to
remain solvent and continue providing world-class response times.
The political decision to reject this subsidy and municipalize the service completely eliminated
the quasi-governmental financial buffer. According to the FY2026 municipal budget, the Fort
Worth Fire Department emergency medical services branch now operates with an immense
annual operating cost of 89.2 million dollars. While the city anticipates recovering roughly 65
million dollars through medical billing and an additional 1.7 million dollars through service
contracts with the fourteen surrounding member cities, the system yields an immediate,
unavoidable structural operating deficit of approximately 22.5 million dollars per annum.
This 22.5 million dollar annual deficit is now entirely the responsibility of the Fort Worth
municipal general fund. When compared to the 3.2 million dollar subsidy originally requested to
salvage the highly efficient MedStar system, the city has deliberately taken on a per annum
financial burden that is an order of magnitude—specifically, a 700 percent increase—larger than
the pre-2024 retention model. Furthermore, this calculation only represents the baseline
operating deficit. It does not account for the massive capital expenditure debt required to
replace an estimated nine frontline ambulances annually, nor does it factor in the millions of
dollars in unfunded, long-term pension liabilities the city absorbed by bringing over six hundred
private contractors into the municipal civil service retirement umbrella.

Concluding Intelligence Assessment
The forensic data explicitly demonstrates that the dissolution of MedStar and the creation of a
fire-based emergency medical system in Fort Worth was driven by administrative ambition and
aggressive labor union mechanics, rather than by clinical necessity or sound economic theory.
By abandoning a highly optimized, dynamically deployed Public Utility Model, the municipality
triggered a cascading, predictable operational failure.
The immediate indicators of systemic collapse are irrefutable. The department is suffering from
an unsustainable 16 percent attrition rate fueled by daily mandated force-hiring. It is failing to
meet critical eight-minute clinical response benchmarks, directly endangering the public. It is
experiencing a logistical failure so profound that frontline medical professionals are forced to
sleep on cots in unpartitioned bays. Furthermore, the geographic reality of attempting to service
a four-hundred-and-thirty-six square mile radius utilizing a rigid, three-hundred-and-fifty square
mile fire station methodology guarantees that geographic efficiency will continue to degrade.
Ultimately, the city manufactured an operational crisis to solve a minor 3.2 million dollar financial
gap, resulting in a system that is demonstrably slower, chronically understaffed, massively more
expensive to the taxpayer, and functionally hostile to the medical personnel attempting to
sustain it.
