Executive Summary
The United States Emergency Medical Services (EMS) system currently exists in a state of
profound structural dissonance, a condition exacerbated by sixty years of misclassification,
bureaucratic neglect, and internecine labor conflicts. Unlike its public safety
counterparts—law enforcement and fire suppression—EMS possesses no unified federal
home, no guaranteed tax base in many jurisdictions, and no distinct professional identity
recognized uniformly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics. It is a profession stranded between
the clinical demands of healthcare and the logistical framework of transportation.
This report serves as a "Deep Dive" into the systemic failures that have brought US EMS to the
brink of collapse in 2025. It traces the lineage of dysfunction from the "Original Sin" of the
1966 Accidental Death and Disability White Paper, which tethered EMS to the Department of
Transportation (DOT), to the modern dissolution of high-performance Public Utility Models
(PUM) like Fort Worth’s MedStar in favor of fire-based hegemony.
Furthermore, this analysis interrogates the active legislative battles of the 119th
Congress—specifically H.R. 3791, H.R. 3443, and H.R. 2220—which seek to rectify decades of
data suppression and reimbursement misalignment. These bureaucratic struggles are
juxtaposed against a rapidly militarizing domestic landscape: the 2025 executive designation
of Fentanyl as a Weapon of Mass Destruction (WMD), the renaming of the Department of
Defense to the "Department of War," and the imposition of draconian digital surveillance laws
in states like Wisconsin and Michigan that threaten the encrypted communications
infrastructure vital to modern telemedicine.
The findings suggest that without a radical federal reclassification of EMS as an essential
healthcare service—decoupled from transportation metrics and shielded from the labor
monopolization of fire unions—the system faces a catastrophic "exodus" of clinical talent to
the nursing sector and a collapse of prehospital care capacity in rural and urban centers alike.



Section I: The Anatomy of the Original Sin — 1966 and
the Department of Transportation
The structural incoherence of modern American EMS is not an accident of evolution but the
direct result of a specific policy decision made in the mid-20th century. To understand why a
paramedic in 2025 cannot bill for treating a diabetic patient on-scene without transport, one
must return to the geopolitical and industrial context of 1966.
1.1 The Neglected Disease of Modern Society

In 1966, the National Academy of Sciences/National Research Council (NAS/NRC) published
the seminal white paper, Accidental Death and Disability: The Neglected Disease of Modern
Society.1 This document is widely revered as the birth certificate of modern EMS, yet it
contains the genetic code for the system’s current dysfunction.
The authors of the White Paper were motivated by a stark statistical reality: in 1965, 52 million
accidental injuries killed 107,000 Americans, causing a societal cost of approximately $18
billion (roughly $129.4 billion in adjusted dollars).3 Motor vehicle crashes accounted for nearly
half of those deaths. The disparity between military and civilian survival rates was glaring; a
soldier wounded in the jungles of Vietnam had a higher statistical probability of survival than a
motorist injured on a California freeway.2 The military possessed a vertically integrated system
of rapid evacuation, field stabilization, and definitive care; the civilian world relied on a
patchwork of funeral home hearses, volunteer rescue squads, and law enforcement wagons
with no medical equipment.2
The White Paper identified five pillars for trauma reduction, modeled on the Haddon Matrix:
safer people, safer roads, safer vehicles, safer speeds, and post-crash care.1 It was this final
pillar—post-crash care—that necessitated the creation of a formal EMS system. However, the
framing of the problem largely determined the solution. Because the primary vector of trauma
was the automobile, the legislative response—the National Highway Safety Act of
1966—placed the nascent EMS apparatus under the jurisdiction of the Department of
Transportation (DOT), specifically the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration
(NHTSA).1
1.2 The "Cargo" Paradigm vs. Clinical Care

The placement of EMS within the DOT established a "transportation-centric" identity that
persists to this day. Under the DOT model, the ambulance is viewed legally and financially as a
vehicle of transport—a specialized form of shipping—rather than a mobile clinical unit.
The Reimbursement Trap:
This classification rippled into the reimbursement structures established by the nascent
Medicare and Medicaid programs. Because EMS was housed under DOT, the Centers for
Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) codified payment based on the transport of a patient to
a hospital. The "service" rendered was defined as the movement of the patient, not the
medical assessment or intervention provided.1
  ●​ The "You Call, We Haul" Incentive: This funding mechanism created a perverse
      incentive known as the "load and go" model. It financially discouraged on-scene
      stabilization, treat-and-release protocols, or transport to alternative destinations (such
      as urgent care or mental health facilities). If the wheels of the ambulance did not turn,
      no revenue was generated.5
   ●​ Cost of Readiness Ignored: Unlike fire departments or police forces, which are funded
      to be ready regardless of call volume, EMS reimbursement only covered the active
      transport phase. This left the "cost of readiness"—the expense of having a fully staffed,
      equipped ICU on wheels waiting for a call—unfunded by federal insurance programs.5
This misalignment has created a catastrophic bottleneck in modern healthcare. As noted in
current analyses, the inability to bill for non-transport services forces EMS agencies to
transport non-emergent patients to already overcrowded Emergency Departments (EDs). This
contributes to "wall time"—the period where an ambulance crew is stuck at the hospital
waiting to offload a patient—which strips communities of ambulance coverage and increases
burnout among crews.5
1.3 The Lost Opportunity of HEW/HHS

The structural flaw was identified almost immediately. In 1972, a follow-up NAS/NRC report,
Roles and Resources of Federal Agencies in Support of Comprehensive Emergency Medical
Services, explicitly criticized the federal fragmentation. It urged the integration of all EMS
efforts into the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (DHEW), the precursor to the
Department of Health and Human Services (HHS).7
The report argued that EMS was fundamentally a medical practice, not a highway safety
measure. While the EMS Systems Act of 1973 briefly created a categorical grant program
within DHEW to foster regional systems, this was dismantled by the Omnibus Budget
Reconciliation Act of 1981, which converted EMS funding into block grants.7 This devolution
allowed states to deprioritize EMS, cementing the DOT as the only federal agency with a
consistent, albeit limited, mandate (highway safety standards) for EMS.
Consequently, EMS lacks a Cabinet-level advocate for its clinical mission. While police have
the Department of Justice and fire services have the U.S. Fire Administration (under
FEMA/DHS), EMS remains a stepchild of the highway administration, fighting for scraps of
funding tied to asphalt rather than patient outcomes.4


Section II: The "Neither/Nor" Identity Crisis — Fire vs.
Third Service vs. Private
The regulatory vacuum left by the federal government allowed three distinct models of EMS
delivery to emerge, creating a fractured labor market and intense inter-agency rivalry. The
primary conflict today exists between the Fire-based EMS model (supported by the
International Association of Fire Fighters - IAFF) and the "Third Service" or Public Utility Model
(PUM).
2.1 The Rise of Fire-Based EMS Hegemony
As structural fires in the United States declined precipitously due to improved building codes
and fire prevention materials, municipal fire departments faced a crisis of relevance and
budget justification.9 To preserve staffing levels and funding, fire departments aggressively
expanded into EMS.
  ●​ The "Dual Role" Model: In this system, personnel are cross-trained as
      firefighter/paramedics. The argument is one of efficiency: a standing army of firefighters
      can be utilized for medical calls.10
  ●​ The "Cash Cow" Critique: Critics, including independent EMS advocates and labor
      analysts, argue that EMS has become the "cash cow" for fire departments, justifying
      multi-million dollar budgets and heavy apparatus for calls that are 90% medical.9
  ●​ The FLSA "80/20" Battle: A critical, often overlooked aspect of this history involves the
      Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA). Firefighters enjoy a partial exemption from overtime
      rules (Section 7(k)), allowing for longer shifts without immediate overtime pay. In the
      1990s, legal battles (e.g., West v. Anne Arundel County, Alex v. City of Chicago)
      challenged whether cross-trained paramedics doing mostly medical work qualified for
      this exemption. The Department of Labor eventually ruled that as long as they are
      trained in fire suppression and have the legal authority to fight fires, they qualify,
      regardless of how much time is spent on EMS.11 This solidified the financial viability of
      the fire-based model by keeping labor costs artificially lower than if they were subject
      to standard 40-hour workweeks.
2.2 Case Study: The Fall of MedStar and the Fire Takeover

The most significant recent battle in this structural war occurred in Fort Worth, Texas, with the
2024-2025 dissolution of MedStar Mobile Healthcare in favor of a fire-based system. This
case serves as a bellwether for the future of the Public Utility Model.
The Rise of MedStar:
MedStar was a classic Public Utility Model (PUM) or "Third Service" agency. It operated
independently of the police and fire departments, solely focused on clinical care and
transport. For decades, it was regarded as a high-performance system, utilizing data to
deploy ambulances dynamically (System Status Management) rather than tethering them to
static fire stations.12
The Collapse (2024-2025):
In May 2024, the Fort Worth City Council voted to dissolve MedStar and transfer EMS
responsibilities to the Fort Worth Fire Department (FWFD).12
   ●​ Financial Unsustainability: MedStar operated on a fee-for-service model. With
       declining reimbursement rates from Medicare/Medicaid and a rise in uninsured patients,
       the independent model became financially precarious. The city refused to subsidize the
       independent authority but was willing to inject tax dollars into its own fire department to
       take over the service.12
   ●​ Union Strategy (Local 440): The transition was heavily championed by IAFF Local 440.
       The union argued that integrating EMS into the fire department would provide civil
       service protection and better benefits for paramedics, who would transition from
      private/quasi-public employment to sworn city positions.14 The union utilized GIS studies
      to argue that fire station placement offered better coverage, despite the static nature of
      stations versus dynamic posting.14
  ●​ The "Single Role" Compromise: Unlike many fire systems that require all staff to fight
      fires, the Fort Worth model created a "single role" civilian EMS classification within the
      fire department. However, friction immediately arose regarding dispatchers. The union
      pushed for dispatchers to be sworn firefighters, arguing they are better equipped for
      stress, while the city aimed for civilian dispatchers to save costs. This highlights the
      continuous struggle for union expansion into all facets of emergency response.16
Operational Consequences:
The transition, officially completed in July 2025, came with a $10 million price tag and
immediate operational degradation.
  ●​ Response Time Lag: Post-transition data from late 2025 indicates that the fire-based
      system is hitting its 90% response time target at 11 minutes and 9 seconds,
      significantly slower than the 8-minute goal and 30 seconds slower than MedStar’s
      performance the previous year.17 Fire officials attribute this to "growing pains" and new
      systems, but it underscores the loss of the PUM's specialized focus on deployment
      logistics.
  ●​ Cultural Absorption: Over 600 MedStar employees were absorbed into the fire
      department. While this preserved jobs, it effectively eliminated an independent,
      medically-focused voice in the region, subsuming EMS culture into the paramilitary
      culture of the fire service.18
2.3 The Private Sector and "High Performance" Reality

While the fire service consolidates power, the private sector (represented by companies like
AMR/GMR) continues to operate largely in the "safety net" or interfacility transport space.
  ●​ The Profit Motive: Private EMS is driven by contract compliance and profit margins.
      This often leads to lower wages for providers compared to fire or third-service
      agencies, fueling high turnover.20
  ●​ Agility vs. Stability: Private companies offer municipalities the ability to outsource risk
      (liability, billing, staffing) but at the cost of control. When a private provider fails or goes
      bankrupt, the municipality is left scrambling, often leading to a revert-to-fire scenario.21

Section III: Current Legislative Battles — The Fight for
Recognition and Data
The federal neglect of EMS has spawned a flurry of legislative attempts to fix the "Original
Sin" through piecemeal bills. These bills, active in the 119th Congress (2025-2026), highlight
the desperate need for data parity and reimbursement reform.
3.1 H.R. 3791: The EMS Counts Act

Objective: To require the Secretary of Labor to revise the Standard Occupational
Classification (SOC) System to accurately count EMS practitioners.22
The Problem: Currently, the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) misclassifies thousands of EMS
providers. A firefighter who is cross-trained as a paramedic is often counted solely as a
firefighter. This results in a "chronic undercount" of the EMS workforce, masking the severity
of the shortage and skewing federal grant allocations.24
  ●​ Sponsors: Rep. Glenn Thompson (R-PA) and Rep. John Mannion (D-NY).22
  ●​ The Coalition: The bill has rare bipartisan support and is endorsed by both NAEMT
     (National Association of Emergency Medical Technicians) and IAFF. For the IAFF, this bill
     is strategic: it ensures their members (dual-role providers) are recognized for their
     medical contributions without stripping them of their firefighter status, which is crucial
     for their retirement and benefit structures (often more generous than civilian EMS).24
3.2 H.R. 3443: Modernizing EMS Delivery Act (The "MEDS" Bill)

Objective: To amend the Social Security Act to create a model for Medicare reimbursement
that includes "Treatment in Place" (TIP) and accounts for the cost of readiness.27
The Transformation: This bill attacks the DOT legacy of "transport-only" reimbursement. It
proposes:
  1.​ Lump Sum Payments: Moving away from fee-for-service toward monthly/quarterly
       payments to maintain readiness. This acknowledges that the value of EMS is availability,
       not just the ride. It specifically includes costs for maintaining supplies of blood
       products and essential medications, which are often in shortage.27
  2.​ Treatment in Place (TIP): Allowing reimbursement for care provided on-scene without
       transport to a hospital. This is critical for reducing ED overcrowding ("wall time") and
       utilizing community paramedicine to treat chronic conditions or minor injuries at home.27
  3.​ Medical Direction: It specifically mandates a study on the value of EMS physician
      oversight. The National Association of EMS Physicians (NAEMSP) strongly supports this,
      arguing that physician oversight is the only way to ensure quality in a modernized,
      clinically focused system.29
3.3 H.R. 2220: The PARA-EMT Act

Objective: To address the workforce shortage by streamlining the transition of military
medics into civilian EMS roles.30
Context: With a 20-30% annual turnover rate in civilian EMS, this bill seeks to utilize veterans
to plug the gap. It provides grants to states to expedite licensure for veterans with military
medical training, bypassing redundant training requirements.30 This aligns with the 2025
geopolitical context of an increasingly militarized domestic preparedness stance, creating a
pipeline from the "Department of War" to civilian streets.
3.4 The Lobbying Ecosystem

The lobbying landscape is a triad of competing interests:
  ●​ IAFF (Fire): The dominant force. They focus on protecting the fire-based model,
      securing SAFER grants (Staffing for Adequate Fire and Emergency Response), and
      ensuring dual-role benefits. They spend significantly on lobbying to ensure EMS remains
      integrated with fire suppression, viewing independent EMS as a threat to their staffing
      models.32
  ●​ NAEMT (Practitioners): Represents the clinical workforce. Their primary push is for the
      "EMS Counts Act" and workforce retention, arguing that EMS is a distinct profession,
      not a subset of fire or a stepping stone to nursing.33
  ●​ AAA (Ambulance Association): Represents private ambulance companies. They lobby
      heavily for reimbursement reform (H.R. 3443) because their profit margins are
      decimated by the current Medicare rate structure, which often pays below the cost of
      service.34


Section IV: The "Nuclear" Threat — Fentanyl, WMDs,
and the Department of War
The legislative and structural battles described above are taking place against a backdrop of
radical geopolitical shifts in 2025. The reclassification of the drug war into a literal war has
profound implications for EMS operational doctrine and liability.
4.1 The Designation of Fentanyl as a WMD

On December 15, 2025, President Donald Trump signed an Executive Order designating illicit
fentanyl and its core precursor chemicals as Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD).35
  ●​ Rationale: The order argues that fentanyl is "closer to a chemical weapon than a
      narcotic," citing its lethality in trace amounts (2mg) and its potential for weaponization
     by terrorists.35 The definition includes "core precursor chemicals" such as Piperidone,
     broadening the scope of interdiction.
  ●​ Operational Shift: This moves the fentanyl crisis from a Public Health Emergency
     (HHS/CDC domain) to a National Security Threat (DHS/DOD domain). For EMS, this
     changes the Rules of Engagement. An overdose call is no longer just a medical
     emergency; it is potentially a HazMat/WMD scene requiring higher levels of Personal
     Protective Equipment (PPE), decontamination protocols, and coordination with
     specialized threat assessment units.35
4.2 The "Department of War"

In a parallel move, the Department of Defense was renamed the Department of War (DOW)
via Executive Order on September 5, 2025.38 While largely symbolic in title, the rhetoric of
"readiness and resolve" and "maximum lethality" signals a shift in domestic posture.
   ●​ Secretary Pete Hegseth: The new Secretary of War, Pete Hegseth, has emphasized a
      culture shift away from "tepid legality" toward "violent effect".39 This aggressive stance
      has already manifested in reports of "kill everyone" orders and "double tap" strikes on
      drug boats in the Caribbean and Eastern Pacific, blurring the lines between military
      action and law enforcement.40
   ●​ Militarization of EMS: With the domestic drug war now framed as a WMD conflict,
      civilian EMS may find itself increasingly integrated into quasi-military operations. The
      NAEMSP and other medical direction bodies will face pressure to align protocols with
      TCCC (Tactical Combat Casualty Care) standards not just for SWAT, but for routine drug
      interdiction responses.
   ●​ Funding Implications: If fentanyl is a WMD, EMS agencies may be able to access
      Department of War or Homeland Security funding streams previously reserved for
      anti-terrorism, bypassing the stagnant DOT block grants. However, this comes with the
      condition of militarized training and compliance.37
4.3 The Legal Quagmire

The designation of fentanyl as a WMD raises immense legal questions for EMS. If a paramedic
enters a "WMD scene" (a fentanyl lab or overdose site) without WMD-level protection, is the
agency liable? Furthermore, the aggressive posture against "narco-terrorists" implies a
battlefield operational environment. The Washington Post has reported on potential war
crimes allegations involving the Secretary of War's directives, suggesting that domestic
support operations could carry significant legal exposure for participating agencies.41


Section V: The Exodus and the Digital Enclosure
The structural failures (DOT), labor conflicts (Fire vs. Private), and escalation of risks (WMD)
have culminated in a massive workforce exodus, complicated by a new layer of digital
restrictions.
5.1 The Great Resignation and the Nurse Pipeline

The EMS workforce is hemorrhaging talent at an unsustainable rate.
  ●​ Turnover Rates: The 2024 Ambulance Employee Workforce Turnover Study found that
     annual turnover for EMTs and paramedics ranges from 20-30%, meaning an agency
     effectively replaces its entire workforce every 3-4 years.42 In New York State, the number
     of active EMS responders declined by 17.5% between 2019 and 2022.43
  ●​ The Wage Gap: The primary driver is economic. In 2025, the median salary for a
     paramedic is approximately $58,410, while a Registered Nurse (RN) earns $93,600—a
     gap of over $35,000 annually.44
  ●​ The "Bridge" Phenomenon: EMS is increasingly viewed not as a career but as a
     stepping stone. Paramedic-to-RN "bridge" programs are proliferating, allowing medics
     to leverage their experience for expedited nursing degrees. Medics are leaving the
     streets for the hospital, where they earn significantly more for safer working conditions,
     better hours, and professional respect.44
  ●​ Burnout: 73% of EMS providers report experiencing burnout or compassion fatigue,
     driven by forced overtime, sleep deprivation, and the moral injury of a failing system.43
5.2 The Digital Enclosure: VPN Bans and App Store Laws

Just as the physical environment for EMS becomes more dangerous (WMDs), the digital
environment is becoming more restrictive, impacting the ability of independent EMS to
operate efficiently.
The VPN Bans (Wisconsin AB 105 / Michigan HB 4938):
Proposed legislation in Wisconsin and Michigan seeks to ban or heavily restrict the use of
VPNs to prevent minors from bypassing age verification on adult sites.45
   ●​ Technical Impact: These bills would force ISPs to block "encrypted tunnels" or require
      websites to block traffic from known VPN IP ranges. For EMS, this is catastrophic.
      Modern EMS relies on SASE (Secure Access Service Edge) and corporate VPNs to
      transmit HIPAA-compliant patient data (EKGs, stroke scales, patient history) from the
      ambulance to the hospital.
   ●​ The Telehealth Threat: Rural EMS relies on telemedicine to connect paramedics with
      doctors for "Treatment in Place." If VPN technologies are criminalized or technically
      obstructed by state firewalls, the secure data pipeline required for these advanced
      consults collapses.46
The Texas App Store Accountability Act (SB 2420):
Effective January 1, 2026, this law requires strict age verification and parental consent for
every app download by a minor, and imposes strict liability on "developers".48
   ●​ Operational Friction: While aimed at social media, the broad definition of "developer"
      and the requirement for "commercial reasonable methods" of age verification creates
      hurdles for EMS agencies using "Bring Your Own Device" (BYOD) policies. If a young
      EMT (under 18) needs to download a protocol app or scheduling tool, the friction of
      parental consent and verification creates administrative chaos.
  ●​ The "Permissioned" Internet: This law represents a shift toward a digital environment
     where anonymity is erased. For EMS, which deals with sensitive patient data, the forced
     collection of ID data by third-party app stores introduces new vector for data
     breaches.50


Section VI: Future Forecast — Best and Worst Case
Scenarios
The convergence of the 1966 structural flaw, the 2025 militarization, and the workforce
collapse points to a bifurcation of futures.
6.1 Worst Case Scenario: The Militarized Collapse

  ●​ Fragmentation: H.R. 3443 (MEDS Act) fails to pass due to budget hawkishness.
     Reimbursement remains tied to transport.
  ●​ Ambulance Deserts: Independent and private EMS agencies go bankrupt. Rural
     America becomes a vast "ambulance desert" where response times exceed 45
     minutes.31
  ●​ Total Fire Takeover: In urban centers, Fire-based EMS (empowered by IAFF) absorbs
     all remaining third services (like MedStar). Costs skyrocket due to pension obligations,
     but clinical innovation stagnates under the "single role" civilian model which treats
     medics as second-class citizens within the fire house.
  ●​ WMD Liability: A fentanyl mass-casualty event occurs. EMS providers, lacking
     military-grade WMD training, refuse to enter the "hot zone" or suffer casualties. The
     "Department of War" deploys National Guard medical units to police domestic streets,
     effectively militarizing prehospital care permanently.
  ●​ Digital Dark Age: VPN bans in key states force EMS agencies to revert to radio and
     paper charting to avoid liability, slowing down care and increasing medical errors.
6.2 Best Case Scenario: The Healthcare Integration

  ●​ Legislative Victory: H.R. 3791 passes, finally counting EMS providers accurately. This
     data forces Congress to pass H.R. 3443, decoupling payment from transport.
  ●​ The Federal Shift: Recognizing the WMD threat, the Executive Branch moves the
     medical oversight of EMS from DOT to a new joint office between HHS and the
     Department of War (for threat defense). This unlocks healthcare dollars for "Treatment
     in Place."
  ●​ Professionalization: Wages rise to match nursing parity due to increased
     reimbursement. The "Para-EMT Act" successfully pipes veterans into the system,
     stabilizing the workforce.
  ●​ Exemptions: Legislators in TX, WI, and MI carve out broad exemptions for healthcare
     and public safety in digital ID/VPN laws, allowing secure EMS data networks to flourish.
6.3 Correlating the Forecast

The trajectory will likely be determined by the tension between the "Department of War"
mindset (centralized, tactical, force-oriented) and the "Digital Enclosure" laws (state-level,
restrictive, privacy-eroding).
  ●​ If the Federal Government (Department of War) asserts dominance, it may override
       state VPN bans for national security personnel (including EMS), creating a two-tier
       internet where first responders have access that civilians do not.
  ●​ The Texas ID/App Store laws serve as a harbinger of a "permission-based" internet.
       For EMS, this means the end of "bring your own device" (BYOD) policies. Agencies will
       be forced to issue government-controlled hardware to bypass age-verification/VPN
       blocks, further centralizing control and increasing costs.
Conclusion
The US EMS system is suffering from a congenital defect—its 1966 placement in the
Department of Transportation—which has metastasized into a systemic failure. Treated as a
shipping industry by the federal government, a revenue stream by fire departments, and a
tactical unit by the new Department of War, EMS has lost its core identity as a healthcare
practice. Without immediate legislative correction (H.R. 3443) and a unified federal home that
prioritizes patient care over tire rotation, the system will not survive the twin pressures of the
fentanyl WMD crisis and the great workforce exodus.
