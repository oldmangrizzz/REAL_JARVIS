The Autopsy of an Anomaly: A Forensic
History of MedStar Mobile Healthcare
(1986–2024)
Introduction: The Death of the Public Utility Model
The formal dissolution of the Metropolitan Area EMS Authority—better known to the world as
MedStar Mobile Healthcare—and its subsequent absorption into the Fort Worth Fire Department
is not merely a change in municipal stationery. It is the violent closing of a chapter in American
medical history. For thirty-eight years, Fort Worth, Texas, served as the primary laboratory for
one of the most contentious, efficient, and clinically innovative experiments in prehospital care:
the Public Utility Model (PUM).
While the victors—the proponents of the fire-based EMS delivery model—will undoubtedly
frame this transition as a consolidation of public safety resources, a forensic examination of the
agency’s history reveals a far more complex narrative. MedStar was an entity born from the
catastrophic failure of the free market in the 1980s, raised on the ruthless economic theories of
Jack Stout, and forced by near-bankruptcy in 2009 to invent a new sector of healthcare that the
world now calls Mobile Integrated Healthcare (MIH).
This report serves as the unvarnished obituary of that experiment. It rejects the sanitized press
releases of the transition in favor of a deep-dive investigation into the structural DNA of the
agency. We will dissect the "reliability crisis" that birthed it, the "system status management" that
defined it, the financial desperation that drove its greatest innovations, and the political gravity
that ultimately killed it. This is the story of how a system designed to treat emergency medicine
as a utility—cold, calculated, and efficient—inadvertently discovered the human side of
healthcare, only to be dismantled by the city that built it.

Part I: The Origin Story (The Chaos of the 1980s)
1.1 The Market Failure and the "Wild West"
To understand the creation of MedStar in 1986, one must first exhume the corpse of the system
that preceded it. In the late 1970s and early 1980s, American EMS was largely an unregulated
frontier. Following the federal impetus of the 1966 "White Paper" (Accidental Death and
Disability), money flowed into the sector, but operational control in many cities, including Fort
Worth, was left to private enterprise.
The prevailing economic theory of the time was that competition would drive quality. If multiple
private ambulance companies competed for calls, response times would theoretically drop, and
patient care would improve. The reality in Fort Worth, however, was a "reliability crisis" of
catastrophic proportions. The private market operated on a mercantile imperative: profit is
derived solely from transport. This created a perverse incentive structure known in the industry
as "claim jumping" or "call jumping."
Ambulance purveyors, fighting for market share, would race to the scene of high-probability
payment calls—motor vehicle accidents in affluent districts or transfers from wealthy
hospitals—while systematically ignoring calls from indigent neighborhoods or during low-volume
hours. The "invisible hand" of the market was not catching the falling patient; it was picking the
pockets of the profitable ones.
By the mid-1980s, the City of Fort Worth faced a grim reality: the private companies were
volatile. When profit margins thinned due to rising fuel costs or changes in Medicare
reimbursement, these companies would threaten to pull out of the city entirely, leaving the
municipality with zero emergency transport capacity overnight. The private market had failed to
provide a safety net. The ambulance was not treated as a public utility, like water or electricity,
but as a commodity, and the citizens were suffering the consequences of this volatility.

1.2 The Jack Stout Philosophy
The City Council, backed into a corner by the unreliability of the private sector and unwilling to
absorb the massive pension liabilities of expanding the Fire Department to cover transport,
turned to a radical economic framework proposed by economist Jack Stout.
Stout was the intellectual father of the Public Utility Model (PUM). His theory was a direct
rejection of both the Fire Department model (which he viewed as bloated and inefficient due to
static deployment) and the unregulated private model (which was predatory and unreliable).
Stout proposed a "Third Way":
   1.​ Separation of Powers: The City would create a quasi-governmental authority (MAEMSA)
       to hold the monopoly license and billing rights. This authority would be the "Regulator."
   2.​ The Contractor: The Authority would not employ the paramedics. Instead, it would bid
       out the operations to a private contractor (over the years, companies like AMR or
       MedStar’s specific operational arm). This contractor would provide the labor and
       management.
   3.​ Performance-Based Contracting: The defining feature of the PUM was the ruthlessness
       of the contract. The contractor was paid based on performance. If an ambulance was late,
       the contractor was fined. If they hit their metrics, they earned profit.
In 1986, Fort Worth adopted this model, passing the "MAEMSA creation ordinance" and forming
an interlocal agreement with surrounding suburbs (including Haltom City, White Settlement, and
others). This moment was the "Big Bang" for MedStar. It was created not as a public safety
agency, but as an administrative tool to enforce market discipline on a chaotic industry.

Part II: The Structure (The "Public Utility Model"
Anomaly)
2.1 The Mechanism of Control: System Status Management
The most distinct operational characteristic of the MedStar era (1986–2024)—and the source of
decades of friction—was its reliance on System Status Management (SSM).
In a traditional Fire Department model, resources are "static." Ambulances and fire trucks are
parked in brick-and-mortar stations. These stations are located based on neighborhood
geography, political districts, and fire risk. They are not necessarily located where medical
emergencies occur. A fire station remains in the same place at 2:00 AM as it is at 2:00 PM,
regardless of where the population has moved.
MedStar, operating under the PUM, rejected the station. Under the guidance of Stout’s theories,
MedStar implemented a "fluid" deployment model.
  ●​ The Data Engine: MedStar analyzed historical data to predict exactly where 911 calls
      would occur at any given hour of the day.
  ●​ The Street Corner Post: Ambulances did not have homes. They were deployed to
      "posting corners"—street intersections that geographically optimized their reach. If the
      data showed a high probability of cardiac arrests in the cultural district at 6:00 PM, an
      ambulance was moved to that specific intersection at 5:45 PM.
  ●​ Unit Hour Utilization (UHU): This is the holy grail metric of the PUM. It measures the
      efficiency of the asset. A UHU of 0.45 means the ambulance is actively working (running a
      call) for 45 minutes of every hour.

2.2 The Friction: Efficiency vs. Reliability
This structural anomaly created a perpetual state of cold war between MedStar and the Fort
Worth Fire Department (FWFD). To the Fire Department, MedStar’s SSM model was
dangerous. They argued that "posting" exhausted crews, who spent 12 hours in a vehicle
without a station to rest, cook, or decompress. They argued that the PUM’s obsession with UHU
meant there was no "surge capacity"—if a mass casualty event occurred, the system was too
lean to absorb the blow.
Conversely, MedStar proponents argued that the Fire model was fiscally irresponsible. Why pay
a crew to sleep in a station for 24 hours when they only run two calls? The PUM was designed
to squeeze every ounce of productivity out of the labor hour.
This tension manifested in the debate over "First Responder Fees." Since Fire Department
units (engines and trucks) often arrived on the scene of medical calls first (due to the density of
stations) but could not transport, the Fire Department historically viewed MedStar as profiting off
their labor. MedStar collected the transport fee (often $1,500+), while the Fire Department,
funded by taxes, received nothing from the billing cycle. This resentment—the feeling that the
PUM was a parasite on the public safety infrastructure—simmered for decades and would
eventually fuel the political arguments for dissolution in 2024.

Part III: The Crucible of Innovation (2009–2014)
3.1 The "Burning Platform"
The historical narrative often romanticizes innovation as the product of genius. In MedStar’s
case, innovation was the product of panic. The narrative that MedStar invented Mobile
Integrated Healthcare (MIH) because they were altruistic visionaries is a fabrication. They
invented it because they were bleeding cash.
In 2008 and 2009, the global financial crisis coincided with a spike in urban 911 utilization.
MedStar’s leadership, specifically Medical Director Dr. Jeff Beeson and operations staff, looked
at the upcoming summer of 2009 with dread. The system was strained to the breaking point.
The "You Call, We Haul" model—the standard since 1966—was failing.
The Audit: To find efficiencies, MedStar conducted a forensic audit of their 911 data. They were
hunting for the "Pareto distribution"—the small number of users causing the most strain. The
results were shocking.
   ●​ The Statistic: The data identified 21 individual patients who had generated nearly 800
       ambulance transports in a single 12-month period.
   ●​ The Financial Hemorrhage: These 800 transports resulted in approximately $1 million
       in billed charges.
   ●​ The Reality: The vast majority of this $1 million was uncollectible bad debt. These
       patients were uninsured, homeless, or suffering from untreated mental illness.
Every time MedStar transported one of these 21 patients, they lost money (fuel, labor, wear and
tear) and removed an ambulance from the system that could have been serving a paying
customer. The realization that 21 people could drive $1 million in distinct costs created the
"burning platform" necessary for radical change.

3.2 The "Light Duty" Hack
The genesis of the solution was serendipitous. MedStar did not have a budget to hire social
workers or case managers. However, they had a "sunk cost": paramedics who were injured and
on "light duty" status. These employees, unable to lift a stretcher due to back injuries or
surgeries, were traditionally relegated to filing papers in the office.
In July 2009, MedStar leadership made a decision that would change global EMS: they sent the
light-duty paramedics to the homes of the 21 super-utilizers.
   ●​ The Logic: "You can't lift a patient, but you can drive a sedan. Go to their house. Sit in
       their living room. Find out why they keep calling 911."
   ●​ The Protocol Drift: This was the birth of the Community Paramedic. It was an
       operational hack. Paramedics, trained to work in 15-minute bursts of adrenaline, were
       suddenly asked to perform forensic social work. They checked pill bottles for interactions.
       They looked for trip hazards (loose rugs). They opened empty fridges.

3.3 The Outcomes and the Skepticism
The results of this "light duty" pilot were statistically impossible to ignore. Among that cohort of
21 patients, 911 utilization dropped by approximately 77%.
  ●​ The Insight: MedStar proved that the "medical emergency" was almost always a "social
      emergency" in disguise. A patient calling 911 for "weakness" was actually calling because
      they hadn't eaten in three days. Once the paramedic fixed the food insecurity, the 911
      calls stopped.
The Pushback: This innovation was not met with universal applause.
  ●​ Regulatory Hurdles: The State of Texas, like most states, defined EMS strictly by
      transport. There was no reimbursement code for "talking to a patient."
  ●​ Scope of Practice Wars: Nurses unions and Home Health agencies initially viewed this as
      "scope creep." They argued that paramedics were not trained in chronic disease
      management. MedStar had to navigate a minefield of regulatory skepticism, arguing that
      they were not replacing home health, but filling the acute gap that occurred at 2:00 AM
      when home health agencies were closed. They had to prove they were "navigators," not
      "practitioners" of primary care.

Part IV: The Business of Saving Money (The
Economics)
4.1 The Economic Inversion
The most difficult hurdle for MedStar was the "Reimbursement Cliff." In 2009-2012, Medicare
and private insurance operated on a fee-for-service model that only paid for transport. If a
MedStar paramedic treated a diabetic at home and didn't take them to the ER, MedStar
generated $0 revenue.
MedStar’s leadership, including CEO Doug Hooten and Chief Transformation Officer Matt
Zavadsky, had to invent a new economic model. They had to convince payers to pay for
non-transport. This was the "Holy Grail" of EMS.

4.2 The CHF Readmission Contracts (JPS Health Network)
The breakthrough arrived via the Affordable Care Act (ACA). The ACA introduced the Hospital
Readmissions Reduction Program (HRRP), which penalized hospitals for excessive
readmissions of patients with Congestive Heart Failure (CHF).
  ●​ The Pitch: MedStar approached JPS Health Network (the county hospital) and Texas
      Health Resources with a proposition: "You are about to lose millions in federal penalties
      because your CHF patients keep bouncing back to the ER. Pay us a flat rate per patient
      to visit them at home, ensure they take their Lasix, weigh them, and keep them out of
      your hospital."
  ●​ The Contract: This was the turning point. MedStar secured contracts where they were
      paid to prevent hospital revenue. For the first time, an EMS agency was financially
      aligned with patient health outcomes rather than transport volume.

4.3 The High Utilizer Group (HUG)
The "21 patients" pilot evolved into the formal High Utilizer Group (HUG) program.
  ●​ Target Demographic: Patients utilizing 911 15 or more times in 90 days.
  ●​ The "Loyalty" Program: Internally, staff referred to this tongue-in-cheek as the "EMS
      Loyalty Program." These patients were "loyal" to the 911 system because every other
      system had failed them.
  ●​ The Human Stories: The program revealed the depth of the cracks in the US healthcare
      system. Patients included the elderly who were simply lonely, the illiterate who couldn't
      read their discharge instructions, and the impoverished who used the ER as a primary
      care clinic. The HUG program provided them with a specific phone number to call instead
      of 911—a direct line to a community paramedic who knew their history.
4.4 The Commercial Payer Breakthrough
MedStar eventually cracked the commercial insurance market (Blue Cross, Aetna, etc.) by
presenting the "Triple Aim" data.
  ●​ The Math: An ambulance transport and ER workup cost the insurer approx. $2,500 -
      $4,000. A community paramedic visit cost approx. $300.
  ●​ The Nurse Triage (ECNS): MedStar implemented the Emergency Communication Nurse
      System, placing RNs in the 911 dispatch center. Low-acuity callers (toothaches, stubbed
      toes) were transferred to the nurse, who could book them a Lyft or Uber to a clinic. This
      saved the insurer the cost of an ambulance and saved the system the capacity of a unit.

Part V: The End of an Era (The 2023-2024 Transition)
5.1 The Dissolution Vote
In May 2024, the Fort Worth City Council voted to dissolve the MAEMSA structure and transition
EMS operations to the Fort Worth Fire Department. This decision marked the death of the
Public Utility Model in Fort Worth and the end of MedStar as an independent entity.

5.2 Why Did the PUM Die? (The Forensic Analysis)
Why would a city dissolve an agency that was a "national model" for innovation? The answer
lies in the convergence of post-pandemic economics and long-standing political friction.
   1.​ Response Time Degradation (Post-COVID): The Achilles heel of the PUM is its reliance
        on a tight labor market. As a private contractor environment, MedStar struggled to recruit
        and retain paramedics during the nationwide labor shortage of 2021-2023. Unlike the Fire
        Department, which offers municipal pensions and civil service protections, MedStar
        offered 401(k)s and high-stress "system status" working conditions. Response times
        slipped, and the "reliability" argument—the very thing that created MedStar in 1986—was
        flipped against it.
   2.​ The Ad Hoc Committee Findings: The City formed an Ad Hoc Committee on
        Emergency Medical Response in 2023. The committee's investigation revealed that the
        subsidy required to keep the PUM floating was increasing. If the city had to subsidize the
        private model heavily, the argument for keeping it independent lost weight.
   3.​ The Political Gravity of Fire: The Fire Association had spent decades arguing that EMS
        is a core public safety function, not a business. With response times suffering, the Fire
        Department presented a plan that offered "stability." They argued that cross-training
      firefighters as paramedics (the firefighter/medic model) provided a depth of resources that
      a private contractor could never match. The political will shifted from "efficiency"
      (MedStar) to "capacity" (Fire).

5.3 The Cost of Transition
The transition is not cheap. The move to a Fire-based model typically involves significantly
higher personnel costs due to civil service pay scales and pension obligations. However, the
City Council viewed this as the price of stability. The PUM, for all its economic brilliance, was
deemed too fragile for the post-pandemic world.

Part VI: Timeline of the Experiment (1986–2025)
  ●​ 1986: The Big Bang. The City of Fort Worth and member cities sign the interlocal
     agreement creating MAEMSA (MedStar) to solve the "reliability crisis" of the private
     ambulance market. Adoption of Jack Stout’s Public Utility Model.
  ●​ 1990s: The Efficiency Era. Implementation of System Status Management (SSM).
     MedStar becomes known for high Unit Hour Utilization (UHU) and low municipal subsidy.
  ●​ 2008: The Crash. Economic downturn puts pressure on municipal budgets. Uninsured
     911 calls spike.
  ●​ July 2009: The Innovation. MedStar launches the "Light Duty" Community Paramedic
     pilot using injured staff to visit 21 super-utilizers.
  ●​ 2010: The Proof. 911 usage among the target group drops 77%. The High Utilizer Group
     (HUG) is formalized.
  ●​ 2012: The Revenue. MedStar signs landmark contracts with JPS Health and Texas
     Health Resources to reduce CHF readmissions, monetizing "non-transport".
  ●​ 2013: The Rebrand. MAEMSA officially changes its trade name to "MedStar Mobile
     Healthcare," signaling a strategic pivot away from pure emergency response.
  ●​ 2014: The Textbook. CEO Doug Hooten and Matt Zavadsky publish Mobile Integrated
     Healthcare - Approach to Implementation, codifying the MedStar model for the world.
  ●​ 2017: The Science. The American Journal of Emergency Medicine publishes
     peer-reviewed outcomes of MedStar’s program, validating the clinical model.
  ●​ 2020-2022: The Strain. The COVID-19 pandemic breaks the labor market. MedStar
     response times suffer due to staffing shortages.
  ●​ 2023: The Audit. The City forms the Ad Hoc Committee on Emergency Medical
     Response to evaluate the future of EMS delivery.
  ●​ May 2024: The Verdict. Fort Worth City Council votes to dissolve the MedStar structure
     and transition operations to the Fort Worth Fire Department.
  ●​ 2025: The Absorption. MedStar operations are fully integrated. The "Teal" ambulances
     are retired. The PUM era concludes.

Part VII: The "Lost" Details (Obituary Notes)
As the Fire Department rewrites the history of EMS in Fort Worth, the following operational
facts—central to the MedStar identity—are at risk of being expunged from the record:
   1.​ The StarSaver Membership: MedStar operated a direct-to-consumer subscription
       program. For a small annual fee ($50-$100), residents were covered for all out-of-pocket
      costs for ambulance transport. This created a direct financial link between the "utility" and
      the "ratepayer," a relationship that does not exist in a tax-funded Fire model. It was a
      vestige of the PUM’s attempt to be a true utility.
  2.​ The "Hope Squad" Culture: MedStar’s shift to MIH required a massive cultural
      re-engineering. Paramedics had to unlearn the "load and go" mentality and learn empathy
      and patience. The "Hope Squad" was an internal peer-support structure created to
      manage the mental health of providers undergoing this high-stress transition. It
      represented the humanization of the workforce.
  3.​ The Granularity of the "21 Patients": The fact that MedStar knew exactly that 21 people
      cost $1 million is a testament to the data-centric nature of the PUM. Most Fire
      Departments, which track "response times" and "fire loss," historically lacked the granular
      billing analytics to even see such a pattern, let alone solve it. This specific data point is
      the tombstone of the agency's analytical capability.
  4.​ The "Teal" Brand: The visual identity of MedStar—the teal and white ambulances—was
      a deliberate choice to distinguish it from "red" (Fire) and "blue" (Police). It signified
      "Healthcare" rather than "Public Safety." The erasure of this livery is the final symbolic act
      of the transition.

Conclusion: The Legacy of a Useful Mutation
The story of MedStar Mobile Healthcare is the story of a "useful mutation" in the evolution of
American medicine. It evolved in the harsh, competitive environment of the 1980s ambulance
wars, developed thick armor in the form of the Public Utility Model, and grew a new
brain—Mobile Integrated Healthcare—during the resource drought of 2009.
MedStar proved that paramedics are not just "ambulance drivers" or "technicians." It proved,
with hard economic data, that they are the mobile eyes and ears of the healthcare system,
capable of solving complex social and medical problems in the living room that hospitals cannot
solve in the ER.
Its dissolution does not negate its success; rather, it highlights the fragility of innovation in the
public sector. The "business of saving money" eventually lost the war to the "politics of public
safety." But for fifteen years, a small administrative agency in Fort Worth, Texas, taught the
world that the best way to save a life is often to sit down at the kitchen table and listen.
